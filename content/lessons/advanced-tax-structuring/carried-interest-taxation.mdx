The tax treatment of carried interest is the most politically visible tax issue in PE globally.

### Current Treatment by Jurisdiction

| Jurisdiction | Carried Interest Tax Treatment |
|-------------|-------------------------------|
| United States | Capital gains rate (20% + 3.8% NIIT) if 3-year hold |
| United Kingdom | Capital gains rate (28% for carried interest from April 2025) |
| Denmark | Depends on structure; may be taxed as income or capital gains depending on the legal form of the carry entitlement |
| Germany | Depends on structure; potential for capital gains treatment under certain partnership structures |
| France | Capital gains with specific conditions; recent reforms have tightened treatment |

### The Arguments

**For capital gains treatment:** Carry is a return on risk-taking. GPs invest their own capital alongside LPs. Carry is only earned after LPs receive their capital back plus a preferred return. It is economically equivalent to an investment return, not a salary. Changing the treatment would distort incentive structures and drive PE activity to more favourable jurisdictions.

**For income treatment:** Carry is compensation for services (managing investments). The GP's personal capital at risk (1–3% of fund capital) is small relative to the carry received (potentially 20% of all profits). No other profession receives such favourable tax treatment on compensation for labour. The current treatment disproportionately benefits the ultra-wealthy.

### Practical Implications

PE professionals should understand that:
- Carried interest tax treatment may change in any jurisdiction at any time
- Structuring carried interest requires specialist tax advice in each relevant jurisdiction
- The political debate is unlikely to be resolved permanently; it resurfaces with each change in government

### Key Terms Introduced in This Module

- **Participation exemption** — a tax exemption for dividends and/or capital gains from qualifying subsidiary shareholdings
- **Thin capitalisation rules** — rules limiting the tax deductibility of interest on excessive related-party debt
- **ATAD (Anti-Tax Avoidance Directive)** — EU directive imposing harmonised anti-avoidance rules including interest limitation
- **Pillar Two** — OECD global minimum effective tax rate of 15% for large multinational groups
- **Section 163(j)** — US Internal Revenue Code provision limiting business interest deductibility to 30% of adjusted taxable income
- **Step-up** — an increase in the tax basis of acquired assets to purchase price, enabling higher depreciation deductions
- **UBTI** — Unrelated Business Taxable Income; US tax concept relevant to tax-exempt LP investors in leveraged deals
- **CFC rules** — Controlled Foreign Company rules requiring a parent company to pay tax on income earned by low-taxed foreign subsidiaries
- **COMI** — Centre of Main Interests; determines which jurisdiction's insolvency (and potentially tax) framework applies
