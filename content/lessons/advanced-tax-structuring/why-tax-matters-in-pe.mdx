Tax affects every stage of a PE transaction:

- **At entry:** How the acquisition is structured (share deal vs. asset deal, holding company jurisdiction) determines interest deductibility, depreciation benefits, and future exit flexibility
- **During the hold period:** Interest deductions on acquisition debt reduce taxable income. Transfer pricing rules govern intercompany transactions. Dividend and interest flows between holding companies and operating companies trigger withholding taxes
- **At exit:** Capital gains tax on the sale of portfolio company shares depends on the holding structure and applicable tax treaties. The difference between a 0% and 25% capital gains rate on a €200M profit is €50M — flowing directly to (or from) LP returns

### The Tax Efficiency Imperative

PE firms do not engage in tax structuring to "avoid" tax in a pejorative sense. They structure transactions to ensure that the same economic outcome is not taxed more heavily than necessary due to suboptimal structuring. A poorly structured cross-border acquisition can result in double taxation (the same income taxed in two jurisdictions) or non-deductible interest (acquisition debt interest that cannot be offset against operating income). Both outcomes reduce returns for LPs.

### Share Deal vs. Asset Deal

The most fundamental structuring decision is whether to acquire the target's **shares** (buying the corporate entity) or the target's **assets** (buying the business assets directly):

**Share deal:**
- The buyer acquires the legal entity, including all assets, liabilities, contracts, and employees
- Simpler (fewer consents required; contracts transfer automatically)
- The buyer inherits the target's existing tax attributes (tax losses, depreciation bases)
- No step-up in asset values for depreciation purposes (in most jurisdictions)
- This is the dominant PE deal structure

**Asset deal:**
- The buyer acquires specific assets and assumes specific liabilities
- More complex (each asset must be transferred; contracts may need consent; employees must transfer under TUPE/Acquired Rights)
- The buyer receives a step-up in the tax basis of acquired assets (allowing higher depreciation deductions)
- More common in US mid-market deals and in certain tax-advantaged situations

The choice depends on the relative tax benefit of the step-up versus the complexity and cost of an asset transfer, which is jurisdiction-specific.
