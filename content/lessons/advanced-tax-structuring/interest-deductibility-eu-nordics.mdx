> **Market-specific: EU/EEA, including Denmark**

### ATAD Interest Limitation (EU-Wide)

The EU Anti-Tax Avoidance Directive (ATAD I, implemented across EU member states from 2019) introduced a harmonised interest limitation rule:

**The rule:** Net borrowing costs (interest expense minus interest income) that exceed **30% of EBITDA** (tax EBITDA) are not deductible for corporate tax purposes. The excess can be carried forward to future years (subject to time limits in some jurisdictions).

**De minimis:** A safe harbour threshold of €3 million of net borrowing costs is available — below this level, the limitation does not apply. Some member states have set higher thresholds.

**Impact on PE:** This rule directly constrains the tax benefit of LBO debt. In a highly leveraged deal where interest expense exceeds 30% of EBITDA, the excess interest is not deductible — increasing the effective tax rate and reducing free cash flow available for debt service.

### Denmark-Specific Interest Limitation

> **Market-specific: Denmark**

Denmark has implemented ATAD's interest limitation with the following specifics:

- **EBITDA limitation:** Net financing costs exceeding 30% of taxable EBITDA are non-deductible. The minimum threshold is DKK 22.3 million (approximately €3 million).
- **Thin capitalisation rules:** If a Danish company's debt-to-equity ratio exceeds 4:1 for controlled debt (debt from related parties), the interest on the excess debt is non-deductible (Selskabsskatteloven § 11).
- **Interest cap rule:** A Danish company cannot deduct net financing costs exceeding 2.0% of its qualifying assets (based on the tax base of certain assets). This "asset-based" limitation operates in addition to the EBITDA-based limitation.
- **CFC rules:** If a Danish parent company controls a foreign subsidiary that is subject to an effective tax rate below 75% of the Danish corporate tax rate (currently 22%), and a significant portion of the subsidiary's income is financial income, the Danish parent may be taxed on the subsidiary's income under CFC rules.

### Practical Impact on Danish PE Deals

A PE firm acquiring a Danish company must model the interaction of these rules carefully. In a typical mid-market Danish LBO:
- Acquisition debt is placed at a Danish holding company (ApS or A/S)
- The holding company lends to the operating company (or the operating company assumes debt directly)
- Interest payments reduce the operating company's taxable income — but subject to the 30% EBITDA cap, thin capitalisation rules (4:1 debt/equity for related-party debt), and the asset-based interest cap
- The tax adviser models the maximum deductible interest under all three limitations and structures the debt to maximise deductibility
