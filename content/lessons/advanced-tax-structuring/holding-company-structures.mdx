PE-backed companies are rarely acquired directly by the fund. Instead, the fund creates a **holding company structure** — a chain of legal entities between the fund and the operating company — designed to optimise tax efficiency, provide legal protection, and facilitate the flow of funds (dividends, interest, sale proceeds) between the operating company and the fund.

### Why Holding Companies Are Used

**Interest deductibility.** Acquisition debt is typically placed at a holding company level (not the operating company level). Interest payments from the operating company to the holding company (via intercompany loans) reduce the operating company's taxable income. The holding company receives interest income, which may be taxed at a lower rate or offset by its own costs.

**Dividend and capital gains efficiency.** Holding company jurisdictions are selected for their tax treatment of dividends received from subsidiaries and capital gains on disposal of subsidiary shares. Ideally, dividends flow tax-free from the operating company to the holding company, and the holding company can sell its subsidiary shares with no or minimal capital gains tax.

**Treaty access.** Holding companies in jurisdictions with broad tax treaty networks can reduce withholding taxes on cross-border dividend and interest payments.

### Common Holding Company Jurisdictions

> **Market-specific note:** The choice of holding jurisdiction depends on the operating company's location, the fund's domicile, and the applicable tax treaties.

**Luxembourg:** Historically the most popular European PE holding jurisdiction. Offers a favourable participation exemption (dividends and capital gains from qualifying subsidiaries are exempt from tax), an extensive treaty network, and a well-developed legal framework for holding structures. However, Luxembourg has come under increased scrutiny from the EU (anti-avoidance directives) and the OECD (BEPS).

**Netherlands:** Similar advantages to Luxembourg. The Dutch participation exemption provides tax-free treatment of qualifying dividends and capital gains. The Netherlands has an extensive treaty network and a favourable advance tax ruling practice.

**UK:** Increasingly used for holding structures, particularly since the UK abolished CFC charges on qualifying holding income and introduced a substantial shareholding exemption (SSE) that exempts capital gains on disposal of qualifying subsidiary shares.

**Denmark:** Danish holding companies benefit from a participation exemption for qualifying subsidiary dividends and capital gains under the Danish Corporation Tax Act (Selskabsskatteloven). However, Denmark has implemented strict interest limitation rules (EBITDA-based limitation at 2.3 million DKK minimum, interest cap at 2% of qualifying assets) and controlled foreign company (CFC) rules that limit the use of low-tax subsidiaries.
