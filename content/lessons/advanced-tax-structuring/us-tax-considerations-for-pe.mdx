> **Market-specific: United States**

### PE Fund Tax Treatment

US PE funds are structured as limited partnerships, which are **pass-through entities** for US tax purposes — the fund itself does not pay tax. Income, gains, and losses are "passed through" to the LPs and GP, who pay tax at their individual or institutional rates.

**Carried interest taxation:** Under current US tax law, carried interest is taxed as long-term capital gains (at a maximum rate of 20% + 3.8% net investment income tax) rather than ordinary income (at a maximum rate of 37%), provided the underlying investment is held for at least 3 years. This favourable treatment has been the subject of ongoing political debate.

### Interest Deductibility (Section 163(j))

US interest deductibility is limited by Section 163(j) of the Internal Revenue Code:

- Net business interest expense is deductible only up to **30% of adjusted taxable income** (similar to the EU ATAD rule)
- Disallowed interest can be carried forward indefinitely
- For PE-backed companies, this limits the tax shield from acquisition debt
- The definition of "adjusted taxable income" was amended in 2022 to exclude depreciation and amortisation (making the limitation tighter than the pre-2022 EBITDA-based calculation)

### Asset Deal Benefits (Section 338(h)(10) / Section 1060)

In the US, asset deals (or elections to treat a share deal as an asset deal under Section 338(h)(10)) provide a **tax basis step-up** — the buyer can depreciate the acquired assets based on their purchase price rather than the seller's historical cost basis. This creates additional depreciation deductions that reduce taxable income over time. The present value of the step-up can be worth 10–15% of the purchase price, making asset deals significantly more attractive in the US than in most other jurisdictions.

### Partnership Tax Complexities

PE funds structured as US partnerships face complex tax issues including:
- **Allocation of income and losses** among LP and GP interests
- **Unrelated business taxable income (UBTI)** — tax-exempt LPs (pension funds, endowments) may be subject to tax on income from debt-financed acquisitions
- **ECI (Effectively Connected Income)** — foreign LPs may be subject to US tax on income effectively connected with a US trade or business
- **FIRPTA** — foreign investors face US tax on gains from the sale of US real property interests
- **State and local taxes** — PE funds and their portfolio companies may be subject to tax in multiple US states
