The **secondaries market** involves the buying and selling of existing PE fund interests and direct company stakes. It has grown into a major asset class in its own right, with over $100 billion in annual transaction volume.

### Types of Secondary Transactions

**LP-led secondaries.** An LP sells its interest in one or more PE funds to a secondary buyer. The buyer acquires a portfolio of underlying companies at a price reflecting the fund's current NAV (often at a discount). The LP gets immediate liquidity; the buyer gets exposure to a diversified PE portfolio at a discount.

**GP-led secondaries (continuation vehicles).** The GP transfers one or more portfolio companies from an existing fund into a new vehicle. Existing LPs choose to cash out or roll their investment. New secondary investors provide fresh capital. Covered in Level 6 (Exit Strategies).

**Direct secondaries.** Buying a direct stake in a PE-backed portfolio company from an existing investor, bypassing the fund structure entirely.

### Why Secondaries Exist

The secondaries market addresses the inherent illiquidity of PE. LPs may need liquidity for various reasons: portfolio rebalancing, regulatory changes, institutional restructuring, or simply because the fund has underperformed and the LP wants to redeploy capital elsewhere.

Buyers benefit from: purchasing at a discount to NAV, gaining visibility into existing portfolios (the assets are already identified, unlike a blind-pool primary fund commitment), and a shortened J-curve (the portfolio is already partially deployed and producing returns).

### Key Secondaries Firms

Ardian, Lexington Partners (acquired by Brookfield), Coller Capital, HarbourVest, Pantheon, and Blackstone Strategic Partners are among the largest secondaries investors globally.
