**Private credit** (also called direct lending or private debt) is the provision of loans directly by non-bank lenders to companies, bypassing traditional bank syndication. Private credit has grown from a niche strategy to one of the largest segments of alternative investments, with over $1.5 trillion in global AUM.

### Why Private Credit Has Grown

**Bank retreat.** Post-2008 banking regulations (Basel III/IV, Dodd-Frank) increased capital requirements for banks holding leveraged loans, reducing their willingness to lend to PE-backed companies, particularly in the mid-market.

**Speed and certainty.** Direct lenders can move faster than bank-led syndication processes. A single direct lender providing a unitranche facility can commit in days; a syndicated bank deal takes weeks or months.

**Flexibility.** Direct lenders offer more flexible terms (covenant-lite structures, PIK options, delayed-draw facilities) than traditional banks.

### Private Credit Return Profile

Private credit funds target returns of 8–15% gross (depending on the risk profile of the lending strategy), with returns generated primarily from interest income rather than capital appreciation. The risk profile is fundamentally different from equity PE: the upside is capped (the borrower repays the loan), but the downside is protected by the security package and the priority claim on assets.

### The Relationship Between PE and Private Credit

PE buyouts and private credit are deeply intertwined:
- PE firms are the primary customers of private credit (leveraged loans finance their acquisitions)
- Many large alternative asset managers (Apollo, Ares, Blue Owl, KKR) manage both PE equity and private credit funds
- Conflicts can arise when a firm's credit fund lends to its own PE portfolio companies
