**Distressed investing** involves acquiring companies or their debt at significant discounts to face value, typically when the company is in or near financial distress (unable to service its debt). **Turnaround** investing involves taking control of a distressed company and restructuring it operationally and financially.

### Distressed Investment Strategies

**Loan-to-own.** Acquire a company's debt at a discount (e.g., 40–60 cents on the dollar), then convert the debt to equity through a restructuring process. The investor becomes the equity owner of a de-leveraged business at a fraction of its enterprise value.

**Distressed-for-control.** Acquire a controlling debt position to gain influence over the restructuring process and ultimately take equity control.

**Trading distressed debt.** Purchase distressed bonds or loans at a discount with the expectation of selling at a higher price once the market recognises the underlying value or the restructuring is completed. This is more of a trading strategy than a traditional PE approach.

### When Distressed Investing Works

Distressed strategies thrive during economic downturns and credit cycle troughs — precisely when traditional buyout activity slows. Firms with dry powder and distressed expertise (Apollo, Cerberus, Oaktree Capital, Centerbridge) deploy aggressively during recessions.

### Case Illustration: Cerberus and Chrysler

Cerberus Capital Management acquired an 80.1% stake in Chrysler from DaimlerChrysler in 2007 for $7.4 billion. The deal was structured as a leveraged buyout, but the timing was catastrophic — Chrysler's sales collapsed during the 2008 financial crisis, and the company filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in April 2009. The US government brokered a restructuring that transferred Chrysler to Fiat (now Stellantis), and Cerberus's equity investment was essentially wiped out. The deal remains one of the most high-profile PE losses in history.
