A **public-to-private (P2P)** transaction, or take-private, is the acquisition of a publicly listed company by a PE firm, resulting in the company's delisting from the stock exchange. Take-privates are among the largest and most complex PE deals.

### Why Take Companies Private?

**Undervaluation.** Some public companies trade below their intrinsic value because the market does not appreciate their long-term potential, they are in an unfashionable sector, or short-term earnings pressure has depressed the stock price. PE firms can acquire these companies at a discount and realise value through patient, long-term ownership.

**Operational transformation.** Some businesses need strategic changes (cost restructuring, management replacement, business model shifts) that are difficult to execute under public market scrutiny. Quarterly earnings pressure, activist investor interference, and media coverage make it hard for public company boards to take short-term pain for long-term gain.

**Capital structure optimisation.** Public companies often have conservative balance sheets (low leverage) because equity investors penalise high debt levels. PE firms can optimise the capital structure by adding leverage — generating higher equity returns while the company is private.

### Take-Private Process

**Approach and negotiation.** The PE firm approaches the target's board with a proposal. The board appoints a special committee of independent directors to evaluate the offer and protect public shareholders.

**Bid premium.** Take-private offers typically carry a 25–40% premium to the undisturbed share price (the price before any acquisition rumour). The premium must be sufficient to win shareholder approval.

**Shareholder vote.** Public company acquisitions require approval by a majority (or supermajority, depending on jurisdiction) of shareholders. In contested deals, proxy advisory firms (ISS, Glass Lewis) and institutional shareholders play significant roles.

**Regulatory approvals.** Antitrust clearance, foreign investment screening, and securities regulatory approvals (SEC in the US, FCA in the UK, BaFin in Germany).

**Financing.** Take-privates require committed financing (debt and equity) before the bid is made public. "Certain funds" provisions (common in UK transactions under the Takeover Code) require the PE buyer to demonstrate that financing is fully committed.

### Case Illustration: Advent International and Cobham

In 2019, Advent International acquired Cobham plc, a UK-listed aerospace and defence technology company, for approximately £4 billion. The deal faced significant public and political opposition — Cobham was a historic British defence company, and critics argued the takeover threatened national security. The UK government intervened, imposing legally binding undertakings on Advent (requirements to maintain Cobham's UK operations, security clearances, and R&D spending). The deal illustrated how take-privates of strategically sensitive companies face regulatory and political hurdles beyond standard M&A process.
