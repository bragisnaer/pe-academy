A **corporate carve-out** is the acquisition of a division, subsidiary, or business unit from a larger corporation. Carve-outs are one of the most reliable deal sourcing channels for PE firms — and among the most complex to execute.

### Why Corporations Sell Divisions

- **Strategic refocusing** — the parent company is divesting non-core businesses to concentrate resources on its primary strategy
- **Portfolio simplification** — conglomerates shedding unrelated businesses to improve stock market valuation (the "conglomerate discount")
- **Underperformance** — the division has underperformed under corporate ownership and the parent has decided it cannot fix the problems
- **Regulatory pressure** — antitrust authorities require divestitures as a condition for approving a separate acquisition
- **Capital allocation** — the parent needs cash for other priorities (debt reduction, share buybacks, acquisitions in the core business)

### Why PE Firms Love Carve-Outs

Carve-outs create natural value creation opportunities:

**Neglected business.** A division competing for capital and management attention within a large corporation often receives neither. Under dedicated PE ownership, the business gets a focused strategy, dedicated management, and investment aligned with its specific needs.

**Cost inefficiency.** Carved-out businesses often carry allocations of corporate overhead (shared services, corporate headquarters costs, IT systems) that exceed the actual cost of providing these functions independently. Replacing corporate allocations with right-sized standalone costs frequently improves margins.

**Entrepreneurial culture.** Moving from a large corporate bureaucracy to PE-backed independent ownership can energise management teams. The combination of equity incentives and operational autonomy can unlock performance that was suppressed under corporate ownership.

### Carve-Out Complexity

**Transitional Service Agreements (TSAs).** The parent typically provides transitional support (IT systems, HR administration, finance and accounting, procurement) to the carved-out business for 12–24 months after closing. Designing, negotiating, and managing TSAs is one of the most complex aspects of carve-out execution. The buyer must exit TSAs before they expire by building standalone capability — a significant project in itself.

**Stranded costs.** Some corporate costs that were allocated to the division do not disappear when the division is sold — they become "stranded" with the parent. Correctly allocating costs between the carved-out business and the parent (determining which costs are "standalone" and which are "allocated") is a major diligence workstream.

**Employee transfer.** Which employees transfer with the business? Are shared-service employees included? Are retention agreements needed for key personnel? In Europe, TUPE and works council requirements add complexity.

**Standalone readiness.** The carved-out business must build capabilities it previously relied on the parent to provide: standalone IT systems, treasury function, tax compliance, legal, HR. The time and cost to achieve standalone readiness must be factored into the LBO model.

### Case Illustration: Siemens and Flender (Carlyle)

In 2020, Carlyle Group acquired an 80% stake in Flender, a German manufacturer of drive technology and gear units, from Siemens for approximately €2 billion. Flender was a non-core business within Siemens's industrial portfolio. Under Carlyle's ownership, Flender gained dedicated management focus, benefited from the wind energy industry's structural growth, and operated as a standalone industrial champion rather than a division competing for attention within a conglomerate. The carve-out required separation from Siemens's IT, procurement, and shared service infrastructure — a multi-year transition programme.
