An **initial public offering (IPO)** lists the portfolio company on a public stock exchange, allowing the PE firm to sell its shares to public market investors over time. IPOs are the least common PE exit route (typically 10–15% of exits) but can produce the highest returns because they access the full premium public markets assign to liquid, diversified businesses.

### The IPO Process for PE-Backed Companies

**Preparation (12–24 months before IPO).** The company must meet public company standards: audited financials under IFRS or US GAAP, independent board members, robust internal controls, and investor-grade reporting capability. PE firms often begin "IPO readiness" preparation 18–24 months before the expected listing.

**Underwriting and roadshow (8–12 weeks).** The PE firm engages investment banks as underwriters. The banks conduct due diligence, draft the prospectus, price the offering, and organise a roadshow where company management presents to institutional investors.

**Pricing and listing.** The IPO price is set based on investor demand during the book-building process. The company lists and begins trading.

**Lock-up period (typically 90–180 days).** The PE firm is contractually restricted from selling shares during the lock-up period after the IPO. After the lock-up expires, the firm sells down its position through secondary offerings or block trades, typically over 1–3 years.

### IPO Considerations

**Advantages:** Access to the public market's premium valuation; ability to retain upside by selling down gradually; the listed company can use its stock as acquisition currency.

**Disadvantages:** Long preparation timeline; significant cost (underwriting fees, legal, accounting, compliance); market risk (if public markets decline between preparation and listing, the IPO may be pulled); ongoing public company obligations.

### Case Illustration: EQT's IPO (2019)

EQT AB, the Nordic PE firm, went public on the Nasdaq Stockholm exchange in September 2019 at a valuation of approximately $8 billion. The IPO allowed EQT's existing owners (including the Wallenberg family's Investor AB) to partially monetise their stakes while giving EQT a listed currency for potential acquisitions. EQT's market capitalisation subsequently grew to over $30 billion at its peak, demonstrating the valuation premium public markets can assign to high-quality alternative asset managers.
