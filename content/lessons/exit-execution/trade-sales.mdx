A **trade sale** (also called a strategic sale) is the sale of a portfolio company to a corporate buyer — a larger company in the same or adjacent industry. Trade sales are the most common PE exit route, typically accounting for 40–50% of all exits by volume.

### Why Strategic Buyers Pay More

Strategic buyers can justify higher prices than financial buyers because they can extract **synergies** — cost savings or revenue enhancements that a standalone financial buyer cannot access:

- **Cost synergies** — eliminating duplicate headquarters, consolidating procurement, merging IT systems, removing redundant staff
- **Revenue synergies** — cross-selling the acquired company's products to the acquirer's customer base (or vice versa), accessing new geographies through the acquirer's distribution network
- **Capability acquisition** — acquiring technology, intellectual property, or talent that would take years to build organically

Because of these synergies, strategic buyers typically pay a **control premium** of 20–40% above the company's standalone valuation. This premium flows directly to the PE fund's returns.

### The Sell-Side M&A Process

PE firms typically engage an investment bank to run a **structured sell-side process** (auction) to maximise competitive tension:

**Phase 1: Preparation (4–8 weeks).** The bank prepares an information memorandum (IM), a management presentation, and a financial model. The PE firm and bank agree on a target buyer list.

**Phase 2: First round (4–6 weeks).** Selected buyers receive the IM and submit non-binding indicative offers (IOIs) based on limited information. The PE firm and bank select a shortlist of bidders to proceed.

**Phase 3: Second round (6–10 weeks).** Shortlisted buyers receive access to the data room, conduct due diligence, meet management, and submit binding final bids with marked-up SPAs. The PE firm selects the winning bidder.

**Phase 4: Closing (4–12 weeks).** SPA negotiation, regulatory approvals, and funding. The deal closes and proceeds are distributed to LPs.

A well-run auction creates **competitive tension** that drives pricing higher than a bilateral negotiation would.
