A **secondary buyout** (SBO) is the sale of a portfolio company from one PE firm to another. SBOs have grown to represent approximately 30–40% of all PE exits, making them the second most common exit route.

### Why PE Firms Buy from Other PE Firms

The growth of SBOs reflects the scale of the PE industry — with over $8 trillion in AUM, PE firms are both the most active buyers and sellers in the M&A market. Specific reasons include:

- The target may be an attractive business that still has significant value creation potential under a different PE strategy (e.g., a new PE owner may fund an international expansion or buy-and-build programme that the first owner did not pursue)
- The selling firm's fund is approaching the end of its life and must exit
- The selling firm may have maximised the value creation levers it specialises in, while the buyer brings different operational capabilities

### SBO Pricing Dynamics

SBOs can present a tension: the buying PE firm needs to acquire at a price that allows target returns (typically 20%+ IRR), while the selling PE firm wants to maximise its exit proceeds. This creates a narrower pricing range than a trade sale, because a financial buyer cannot pay synergy-driven premiums.

However, SBOs offer advantages: PE buyers are experienced acquirers, they can move quickly (reducing execution risk for the seller), and they are familiar with PE-backed company dynamics (leveraged balance sheets, management incentive programmes, reporting standards).

### Continuation Vehicles (GP-Led Secondaries)

A rapidly growing variation of the SBO is the **continuation vehicle** (also called a GP-led secondary). In this structure, the GP moves a portfolio company from an existing fund (approaching the end of its life) into a newly created vehicle:

- Existing LPs can choose to cash out (receiving liquidity) or roll their investment into the new vehicle
- New LPs (typically secondary fund investors) commit fresh capital
- The GP retains management of the asset and continues executing the value creation plan

Continuation vehicles address a fundamental tension in PE: fund life constraints force exits even when the GP believes further value creation is possible. By creating a new vehicle, the GP extends the hold period while providing liquidity to LPs who want it.

The continuation vehicle market has grown from roughly $10 billion in 2018 to over $50 billion annually, driven by LP demand for liquidity and GP desire to retain high-performing assets.
