Running a sell-side M&A process is an art. The objective is to create maximum competitive tension while maintaining process discipline and confidentiality.

### Choosing the Right Adviser

PE firms select sell-side advisers based on:
- Sector expertise (does the bank know the buyer universe in this sector?)
- Execution track record (has the team completed similar transactions recently?)
- Buyer relationships (can the bank credibly reach the most likely strategic and financial buyers?)
- Fee structure (success fees are standard; retainers and minimum fees vary)

For mid-market transactions (EV $100M–$1B), specialist M&A advisers (Houlihan Lokey, William Blair, Lincoln International, Baird, Rothschild) often outperform bulge-bracket banks because of deeper sector focus and more senior banker involvement.

### Dual-Track Process

A **dual-track process** runs an M&A sale process and an IPO preparation simultaneously. The PE firm pursues both exit routes in parallel and selects the one that produces the highest value at the decision point.

Advantages: maximises optionality and creates competitive tension (potential trade buyers know an IPO is a credible alternative, incentivising higher bids).

Disadvantages: expensive (both M&A and IPO workstreams require separate adviser teams), time-consuming, and management attention is split.

Dual-track is common for larger exits ($1B+ EV) where both routes are viable.

### Managing the Bid-Ask Spread

The **bid-ask spread** — the gap between what the seller expects and what buyers will pay — is the central challenge in any exit process. Factors that widen the spread:

- The seller anchors to peak valuations from a prior period
- The buyer uses a more conservative EBITDA figure than the seller
- Credit market conditions have tightened since the seller's expectations were set
- The business has weakened since the seller began preparing for exit

The sell-side adviser's role is to narrow the spread through competitive tension, information provision, and creative deal structuring (earn-outs, deferred consideration, minority stake rollovers) that bridge valuation gaps.
