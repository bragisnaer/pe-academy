An **earn-out** is a deal structure where a portion of the purchase price is contingent on the target business achieving specified performance milestones (typically revenue or EBITDA targets) after closing. Earn-outs bridge valuation gaps when buyer and seller disagree on the business's future performance.

### How Earn-Outs Work

A typical earn-out structure: The buyer pays $200M at closing plus up to $50M in additional consideration if the business achieves specified EBITDA targets over the next 2 years.

### When Earn-Outs Are Used

- **Founder-led businesses** where the founder's continued involvement is critical to customer relationships or business performance
- **High-growth businesses** where the seller believes near-term growth will justify a higher price than the buyer is willing to pay upfront
- **Bridging valuation gaps** when market uncertainty makes the buyer reluctant to pay the seller's full asking price

### Earn-Out Risks

- **Misaligned incentives** — the buyer controls the business post-closing and may make decisions (cost cuts, strategic pivots, pricing changes) that reduce earn-out achievement even if they increase long-term value
- **Accounting disputes** — disagreements about how EBITDA is calculated for earn-out purposes are extremely common and frequently result in litigation or arbitration
- **Management distraction** — the seller (often still involved as management) focuses on short-term earn-out targets rather than long-term value creation

Experienced PE sellers avoid earn-outs when possible because they introduce uncertainty and conflict. When earn-outs are necessary, clear contractual definitions of the measurement methodology are essential.
