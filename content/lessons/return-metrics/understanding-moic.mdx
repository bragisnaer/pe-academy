**MOIC** — Multiple on Invested Capital — is the simplest and most intuitive measure of private equity return. It answers one question: for every dollar invested, how many dollars came back?

A 3x MOIC means $1 invested returned $3. A 0.8x MOIC means the investment lost money — only $0.80 returned for every $1 put in.

MOIC is the first number PE professionals reach for when evaluating a deal's headline performance. It is also the first number LPs quote when comparing fund vintages. Understanding exactly what MOIC measures — and what it misses — is essential for interpreting PE performance data.

## The MOIC Calculation

**Multiple on Invested Capital (MOIC)** is calculated as:

> **MOIC = Total Value Returned ÷ Total Capital Invested**

Total value returned includes:
- All cash distributions received from the investment (dividends, recapitalisation proceeds, exit proceeds)
- The current fair value of any remaining unrealised position

Total capital invested is the cumulative equity contributed to the investment.

### Worked Example

A PE fund acquires a business for $200 million, contributing $80 million of equity and $120 million of debt.

Over 5 years, the fund:
- Receives a $10 million dividend recapitalisation in year 3
- Sells the business in year 5 for $320 million, repaying $90 million of remaining debt and receiving $230 million of equity proceeds

**Total value returned:** $10 million + $230 million = $240 million
**Total capital invested:** $80 million

> **MOIC = $240 million ÷ $80 million = 3.0x**

This is a 3.0x **gross MOIC** — before deducting management fees and carried interest paid to the GP.

## Gross MOIC vs. Net MOIC

PE firms report returns on two bases:

**Gross MOIC** reflects returns at the deal or fund level before fees and carried interest. It shows the raw investment performance — how well the GP picked and managed investments.

**Net MOIC** reflects what LPs actually received after deducting management fees and the GP's 20% carried interest. Net MOIC is always lower than gross MOIC, and it is the number that matters to LPs evaluating fund performance.

The gap between gross and net can be substantial. A fund with a 3.0x gross MOIC might deliver a 2.3x–2.5x net MOIC after fees — meaningful in absolute terms but important context when comparing reported performance across funds.

> **Rule of thumb:** Top-quartile PE funds target 2.5x–3.5x net MOIC over a 10-year fund life. Funds below 1.5x net MOIC are generally considered to have underperformed their public market equivalent.

## Why MOIC Alone Is Insufficient

MOIC is a powerful, intuitive metric — but it has a critical blind spot: **it ignores time**.

Consider two investments, both with a 3.0x MOIC:

- **Investment A:** $10 million invested, $30 million returned after **3 years**
- **Investment B:** $10 million invested, $30 million returned after **10 years**

Both show identical 3.0x MOIC. But Investment A delivered roughly 44% annualised returns, while Investment B delivered roughly 12% annualised returns. In the 7 additional years Investment B was held, the capital could have been reinvested elsewhere.

This is why **MOIC is always used alongside IRR** — the time-adjusted return metric that accounts for exactly when capital was deployed and when it was returned. A high MOIC achieved quickly is far more valuable than the same MOIC achieved slowly.

## MOIC in Fund Reporting

At the fund level, MOIC aggregates across all investments:

- **Realised MOIC** — calculated only on investments that have been fully exited
- **Total Value to Paid-In (TVPI)** — the fund-level equivalent of MOIC, including both realised proceeds and the current fair value of unrealised holdings divided by total capital called
- **Distributed to Paid-In (DPI)** — realised cash returned to LPs as a multiple of paid-in capital; a more conservative measure that excludes unrealised value

DPI is particularly important to LPs because it measures actual cash received, not paper gains. A fund with a high TVPI but low DPI has generated significant unrealised value on paper — but LPs cannot spend paper gains until exits are completed.

## Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **MOIC** — Multiple on Invested Capital; total value returned divided by total capital invested; the primary headline return metric in PE
- **Multiple on invested capital** — the formal name for MOIC; measures absolute investment return independent of time
- **Gross MOIC** — MOIC calculated before deducting management fees and carried interest; reflects raw deal performance
- **Net MOIC** — MOIC after all fees and carried interest; the figure relevant to LP investors
- **TVPI** — Total Value to Paid-In; fund-level equivalent of MOIC including unrealised fair value
- **DPI** — Distributed to Paid-In; cash actually returned to LPs as a multiple of capital contributed; excludes unrealised value
