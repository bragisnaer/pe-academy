IRR — Internal Rate of Return — is the annualised return on a private equity investment, calculated as the discount rate that makes the net present value (NPV) of all cash flows equal to zero, accounting for both the timing and magnitude of each capital call and distribution.

IRR is the primary performance benchmark in private equity because it captures the time value of money — a fast return is worth more than a slow one, which is why PE firms often pursue early exits or dividend recapitalisations to boost reported IRR alongside the J-curve effect.

This lesson explains IRR calculation mechanics, how it relates to MOIC across different holding periods, the difference between gross IRR (deal level) and net IRR (after fees and carry), and why IRR can be manipulated through subscription credit facilities — a growing LP governance concern.
