Emerging market PE — spanning Africa, Latin America, Central/Eastern Europe, and parts of Asia — offers higher growth potential but carries risks that do not exist (or exist to a lesser degree) in developed markets.

### The Opportunity

Emerging markets offer structural growth rates that developed economies cannot match. GDP growth of 4–7% annually, rapid urbanisation, expanding middle classes, and underdeveloped financial infrastructure create large addressable markets for consumer goods, healthcare, financial services, education, and technology.

Many emerging market companies operate in fragmented industries with limited institutional ownership — conditions that favour PE's consolidation and professionalisation playbook.

### The Risks

**Political and regulatory risk.** Government stability, rule of law, and regulatory predictability vary enormously. Changes in government can reverse policies affecting PE investments — tax incentives, foreign ownership rules, repatriation restrictions, or industry regulations.

**Currency risk.** Emerging market currencies are more volatile than developed market currencies. A business generating healthy local-currency returns can produce poor dollar or euro returns if the local currency depreciates during the hold period. For example, a Brazilian portfolio company generating strong real-denominated growth will lose value in USD terms if the BRL/USD exchange rate weakens.

**Exit market depth.** Exit options in emerging markets are more limited: domestic public markets are smaller and less liquid; the strategic buyer universe is narrower; and secondary buyout markets are less developed. IPO windows are less predictable, and cross-border trade sales face additional regulatory hurdles.

**Governance and transparency.** Financial reporting quality, corporate governance standards, and regulatory enforcement vary. Due diligence in emerging markets is more time-intensive and requires local expertise.

### Regional Highlights

**Latin America.** Brazil and Mexico are the largest PE markets. Key sectors: financial technology, healthcare, agribusiness, and consumer. PE activity has grown significantly but faces currency volatility, complex tax systems, and political uncertainty. Key firms: Advent International (Latin America focused), Patria Investments, Vinci Partners.

**Africa.** PE activity is concentrated in South Africa, Nigeria, Kenya, and North Africa. Deal sizes are smaller (typically $10–100M), and growth equity is more common than leveraged buyouts due to limited debt markets. Key sectors: financial services, telecoms, consumer, and agribusiness. Key firms: African Infrastructure Investment Managers (AIIM), Helios Investment Partners, Development Partners International (DPI).

**Central and Eastern Europe (CEE).** Poland, Czech Republic, Romania, and Hungary have developed PE markets with EU regulatory alignment. EU accession has improved governance standards, and the region benefits from lower labour costs and proximity to Western European markets. Key firms: Mid Europa Partners, Enterprise Investors, Abris Capital Partners.

### Case Illustration: Actis and Bujagali Hydropower (Uganda)

Actis, a London-based emerging market PE firm, invested in the Bujagali hydroelectric power plant in Uganda — a 250MW facility that upon completion in 2012 became the largest private power sector investment in sub-Saharan Africa. The project exemplified emerging market infrastructure PE: high development risk, long-term concession revenue, and critical social impact (providing approximately half of Uganda's electricity generation capacity). Actis subsequently exited its stake to the Aga Khan Fund for Economic Development.
