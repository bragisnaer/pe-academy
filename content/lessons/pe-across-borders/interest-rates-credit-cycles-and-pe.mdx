Interest rates are the single most important macroeconomic variable for PE. They affect the cost of acquisition debt, the availability of leverage, asset valuations, and the competitive landscape.

### How Interest Rates Affect PE

**Cost of debt.** When interest rates rise, the cost of LBO financing increases. Higher interest rates reduce the amount of debt a company can service (because more cash goes to interest payments), which means PE firms must contribute more equity to each deal or pay lower prices.

**Valuations.** Lower interest rates generally inflate asset valuations (both public and private) by reducing discount rates and increasing the leverage available to buyers. When rates rise, valuations compress.

**Deal activity.** The most active PE deal environments coincide with low interest rates and easy credit conditions (2005–2007, 2013–2021). Rate rises in 2022–2023 caused a significant slowdown in PE deal volume as the bid-ask spread widened (sellers anchored to peak valuations while buyers adjusted for higher financing costs).

**Exit markets.** Rising rates can shrink exit multiples and reduce the availability of LBO financing for secondary buyout buyers, making exits harder and extending hold periods.

### The Credit Cycle

PE deal activity follows a credit cycle:

**Early cycle (recovery).** Credit markets reopen after a downturn. Spreads are wide but declining. Deal volume picks up as financing becomes available. Valuations are relatively low — often the best entry point.

**Mid-cycle (expansion).** Credit is readily available, spreads are narrow, and deal volume is high. Valuations rise as competition intensifies. Most PE vintage years fall in this phase.

**Late cycle (peak).** Credit is abundant and cheap. Leverage ratios reach peak levels. Valuations are high. Covenant protections for lenders are weakest. This is the most dangerous time to invest — the highest entry prices coincide with the most fragile credit markets.

**Downturn (contraction).** Credit markets freeze or tighten dramatically. Deal volume collapses. Portfolio companies with high leverage face distress. Firms with dry powder can acquire assets at distressed valuations — often producing the best vintage-year returns.

### Case Illustration: The 2022–2024 Rate Shock

The rapid interest rate increases by the US Federal Reserve and European Central Bank in 2022–2023 (from near-zero to 5%+ in the US and 4%+ in Europe) represented the most significant rate shock to PE in decades. The impact was immediate:

- LBO financing costs roughly doubled, compressing the price PE firms could pay
- Deal volume fell approximately 35% from 2021 peaks
- Exit activity slowed dramatically as the bid-ask spread widened
- Portfolio companies with floating-rate debt faced sharply higher interest expenses
- PE fundraising slowed as LPs faced the "denominator effect" (PE allocations appeared oversized relative to falling public market portfolios)

The firms that navigated this period best were those with: conservative leverage in existing portfolio companies, significant dry powder for new deployment, and operational value creation capabilities that did not depend on financial engineering.

### Key Terms Introduced in This Module

- **Mittelstand** — the large base of mid-market, often family-owned businesses in Germany; a key PE acquisition target pool
- **Foreign investment screening** — government review processes for foreign acquisitions of businesses in sensitive sectors (CFIUS in the US, national regimes across the EU)
- **Denominator effect** — the phenomenon where falling public market values cause PE allocations to appear oversized in institutional portfolios, constraining new PE commitments
- **Credit cycle** — the recurring pattern of credit expansion and contraction that drives PE deal volume and valuations
