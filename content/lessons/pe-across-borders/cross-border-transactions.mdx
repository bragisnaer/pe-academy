Cross-border PE deals — where the buyer, seller, target, or financing are in different jurisdictions — add layers of complexity that domestic transactions do not face.

### Key Cross-Border Considerations

**Regulatory approvals.** Cross-border deals often require approval from multiple competition authorities (European Commission, US FTC/DOJ, Chinese SAMR, and national regulators). Foreign investment screening regimes (CFIUS in the US, EU Foreign Subsidies Regulation, national FDI screening across EU member states) can delay or block transactions involving sensitive sectors (defence, technology, critical infrastructure).

**Tax structuring.** PE firms structure cross-border deals to optimise withholding taxes on dividends and interest payments between jurisdictions, capital gains tax treatment at exit, and the deductibility of acquisition debt interest. Transfer pricing rules (ensuring intra-group transactions are conducted at arm's length) and anti-avoidance legislation (BEPS, ATAD in the EU) constrain structuring options.

**Currency risk.** If a fund denominates in USD but acquires a company earning revenue in EUR, the fund bears FX translation risk. PE firms manage this through: natural hedging (matching the currency of debt with the currency of cash flows), financial hedging (forward contracts, options), and portfolio diversification across currencies.

**Cultural and governance differences.** Business practices, negotiation styles, governance expectations, and employee relations vary dramatically across jurisdictions. A PE firm that imposes a US-style governance framework on a Japanese portfolio company without cultural sensitivity will face resistance.
