### Types of Currency Exposure

**Transaction exposure.** A portfolio company buying inputs in one currency and selling outputs in another faces margin risk from exchange rate movements. Example: a German manufacturer (costs in EUR) selling to the UK (revenue in GBP) faces margin compression if GBP weakens against EUR.

**Translation exposure.** The PE fund reports in a base currency (e.g., USD or EUR). Portfolio company valuations in foreign currencies must be translated at current exchange rates for reporting purposes. Currency movements can inflate or deflate reported NAV even if the underlying business performance is unchanged.

**Economic exposure.** Long-term competitive dynamics change with currency movements. A sustained strengthening of a portfolio company's home currency can make its exports uncompetitive, even if the company has no direct foreign currency transactions.

### Hedging Approaches

**Natural hedging (preferred).** Match the currency of debt with the currency of the portfolio company's cash flows. If the company generates EUR revenue, finance it with EUR debt. This eliminates translation exposure on the debt component. Most effective and lowest cost over the multi-year hold periods typical in PE.

**Financial hedging.** Forward contracts and options can lock in exchange rates for specific future cash flows. Effective for known, near-term exposures (a dividend payment in 6 months, a bolt-on acquisition in another currency). Less effective for multi-year exposures because hedging costs compound.

**Portfolio diversification.** A fund with portfolio companies generating revenue in 5–6 currencies is naturally diversified against any single currency movement. This is not a hedge for individual deal returns but reduces aggregate fund-level currency risk.

### Exercise: Currency Impact on Returns

**Task:** A EUR-denominated PE fund acquires a UK company for £100M at a EUR/GBP rate of 1.15 (cost in EUR: €115M). Over 5 years, the company doubles its equity value to £200M. But EUR/GBP has moved to 1.20.

Calculate: (a) exit value in EUR, (b) MOIC in GBP, (c) MOIC in EUR, (d) the currency drag.

**Solution:**
- (a) £200M / 1.20 = **€166.7M** exit value in EUR
- (b) GBP MOIC = £200M / £100M = **2.0x**
- (c) EUR MOIC = €166.7M / €115M = **1.45x**
- (d) Currency drag reduced the MOIC from 2.0x to 1.45x — a 28% reduction in return due to GBP weakness.

This exercise illustrates why currency risk management matters for cross-border PE.

### Key Terms Introduced in This Supplement

- **Transaction exposure** — margin risk from currency movements affecting a company's operating cash flows
- **Translation exposure** — reporting risk from converting foreign-currency valuations to the fund's base currency
- **Natural hedging** — matching debt currency with cash flow currency to eliminate translation exposure
- **BEPS** — the OECD's Base Erosion and Profit Shifting framework limiting aggressive international tax structures
