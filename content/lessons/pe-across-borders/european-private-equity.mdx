Europe is the second-largest PE market globally, with distinct characteristics that differentiate it from the US market. Understanding these differences is essential for anyone working in or allocating to European PE.

### Market Structure

The European PE landscape is diverse, spanning 40+ countries with different legal systems, languages, tax regimes, and business cultures. The largest markets by PE deal volume are:

- **United Kingdom** — the largest European PE market, with London as the continent's financial hub. UK PE benefits from English common law (familiar to international investors), a deep capital market, and extensive advisory infrastructure.
- **Germany** — Europe's largest economy, with a PE market dominated by mid-market deals involving family-owned *Mittelstand* businesses. German deals often involve complex succession planning for multi-generational family enterprises.
- **France** — a large PE market with strong domestic firms (PAI Partners, Eurazeo, Ardian). French labour law and works council requirements add complexity to deal execution and post-acquisition restructuring.
- **Nordics (Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland)** — a remarkably active PE market relative to population size. The Nordics produce a disproportionate number of technology and healthcare companies, and have a strong culture of institutional investing. EQT, Nordic Capital, Altor, IK Partners, and Hg Capital all have deep Nordic roots.
- **Benelux, Italy, Spain, and Central/Eastern Europe** — growing markets with increasing PE activity, often focused on mid-market opportunities and family business successions.

### How European PE Differs from US PE

**Deal sourcing.** A higher proportion of European PE deals involve family-owned businesses, where relationship-driven sourcing and succession planning expertise are critical. Formal auction processes are common for larger deals, but proprietary deal flow matters disproportionately in the mid-market.

**Leverage levels.** European leveraged loans have historically carried lower leverage multiples than US deals (average entry leverage 4.5–5.5x in Europe vs. 5.5–6.5x in the US), reflecting more conservative lending cultures and regulatory oversight. This gap has narrowed in recent years.

**Labour regulation.** European employment law is generally more protective than US law. Works council consultation (mandatory in many EU countries before significant workforce changes), transfer of undertakings regulations (TUPE in the UK, Acquired Rights Directive across the EU), and collective bargaining agreements create additional complexity — and cost — for post-acquisition restructuring.

**Fund structures.** European funds predominantly use whole-fund carry waterfalls (European waterfall), which are more LP-friendly than the deal-by-deal (American) waterfalls common in US funds.

**Currency exposure.** European PE portfolios inherently involve multi-currency exposure (EUR, GBP, SEK, NOK, DKK, CHF). PE firms must manage currency risk at both the fund level and the portfolio company level, particularly for cross-border bolt-on acquisitions.

### Case Illustration: CVC and Breitling

CVC Capital Partners acquired Swiss luxury watchmaker Breitling in 2017, reportedly for approximately CHF 870 million. CVC's thesis centred on brand repositioning, retail expansion (shifting from wholesale to direct-to-consumer), and digital transformation. During CVC's ownership, Breitling significantly expanded its e-commerce presence, opened new boutiques across Europe and Asia, and refreshed its product line. Partners Group subsequently acquired a significant minority stake, valuing the company at over CHF 3 billion — a substantial uplift from CVC's entry price.
