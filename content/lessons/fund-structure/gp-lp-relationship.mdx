The relationship between a **general partner (GP)** and its **limited partners (LPs)** is the defining structural relationship in private equity. It determines who controls capital, who bears risk, how profits are distributed, and how accountability is maintained over a fund's 10-year life.

Understanding this relationship is essential for anyone working in or alongside the PE industry — whether as an investor, a portfolio company executive, an adviser, or a career changer into PE.

## Capital Commitments and the Commitment Period

LPs do not transfer money to the GP when a fund closes. Instead, they make a **commitment** — a legally binding promise to contribute capital when called. The total of all LP commitments equals the fund size.

When the GP identifies an investment, it issues a **capital call** (also called a "drawdown notice") to each LP, specifying:

- The amount being called (each LP contributes their pro-rata share of total commitments)
- The purpose of the call (which investment or operating expense)
- The deadline for funding (typically 10 business days)

LP failure to meet a capital call is a serious breach of the limited partnership agreement (LPA) and typically triggers penalty provisions — including potential forfeiture of the LP's interest in the fund.

> **Example:** A $1 billion fund with 20 LPs each holding a $50 million commitment. When the GP calls $100 million to acquire a company, each LP contributes $5 million (10% of their commitment) within 10 days.

## Distributions and the Waterfall

When portfolio companies are sold or recapitalised, the GP distributes proceeds to LPs. The sequence in which profits are distributed is governed by the **waterfall** — a contractual priority structure defined in the LPA.

A typical American waterfall (deal-by-deal) works as follows:

1. **Return of capital** — LPs receive back their invested capital first (100% to LPs until capital is returned)
2. **Preferred return (hurdle rate)** — LPs receive a preferred return on their capital, typically 8% per year compounded
3. **GP catch-up** — once the hurdle is met, the GP receives 100% of distributions until it has received 20% of total profits to date
4. **Carried interest split** — remaining profits split 80% LP / 20% GP

The European waterfall differs: it requires the GP to return *all* LP capital (not just deal-by-deal capital) before carried interest is paid. This is more LP-friendly and is now common in European and increasingly US fund structures.

**Clawback provision:** If the GP receives carried interest early in a fund's life but later deals underperform, a clawback clause requires the GP to return excess carry to LPs. This protects LPs from paying carry on temporarily positive returns that are later reversed.

## Dry Powder and the Commitment Period

Between capital calls, committed but undeployed LP capital sits in LP accounts (not with the GP). This undeployed capital is called **dry powder** from the LP perspective — capital committed but not yet put to work.

The **commitment period** — typically the first 5 years of the fund — is the window during which the GP can call capital for new platform investments. After the commitment period closes, remaining undrawn commitments can generally only be called for follow-on investments in existing portfolio companies or fund expenses.

## GP Reporting Obligations

In exchange for LP capital commitments, GPs have substantial reporting obligations. Standard quarterly reports include:

- **NAV (net asset value)** — current estimated value of each portfolio company, based on comparable transaction multiples or DCF analysis
- **MOIC and IRR** — current gross and net multiple on invested capital and internal rate of return at the fund level and for individual investments
- **Capital account statements** — each LP's individual capital account showing contributions, distributions, and current fair value
- **Investment activity** — new investments made, exits completed, and significant operational developments at portfolio companies

Annual audited financial statements provide a more rigorous assessment of fund performance.

## The LP Advisory Committee (LPAC)

Most funds establish an **LP advisory committee (LPAC)** — a subset of the largest or most influential LPs who meet periodically with the GP to review matters requiring LP consent. Typical LPAC responsibilities include:

- Approving conflicts of interest (e.g., when the GP wants to invest in a company related to an existing portfolio company)
- Reviewing valuation methodology
- Consenting to extensions of the fund's life or commitment period
- Approving key person provisions (if a named senior partner leaves, the LPAC may trigger a fund review)

The LPAC does not have veto power over investment decisions — that remains with the GP. Its role is governance and oversight, not portfolio management.

## Alignment of Interests

The GP/LP structure is designed to align incentives through two mechanisms:

**GP commit:** GPs invest 1–2% of fund capital alongside LPs. For a $1 billion fund, that is $10–20 million of the GP's own capital at risk. This ensures GPs are not purely fee-driven — their personal wealth is tied to the same outcomes as LP capital.

**Carried interest:** The 20% profit share only materialises if the fund generates returns above the hurdle rate. A fund that earns 4% per year returns nothing in carry. A fund generating 25% annual returns creates enormous carry — creating powerful incentives for GPs to maximise performance.

## Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Capital call** — a GP notice requiring LPs to contribute committed capital to fund an investment; also called a drawdown
- **Distribution** — return of capital or profits from the GP to LPs following a portfolio company exit or recapitalisation
- **Dry powder** — committed LP capital not yet called and deployed by the GP
- **Commitment** — an LP's legally binding pledge to contribute a specified amount to a PE fund when called
- **Waterfall** — the contractual sequence governing how fund profits are distributed between LPs and the GP
- **Clawback** — a provision requiring the GP to return excess carried interest if later fund performance falls below the hurdle rate
- **LPAC** — limited partner advisory committee; a governance body of key LPs that oversees conflicts of interest and fund governance matters
