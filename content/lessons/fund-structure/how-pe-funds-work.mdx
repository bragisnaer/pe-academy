A private equity fund is a closed-end investment vehicle with a defined lifespan — typically 10 years — during which capital is raised from investors, deployed into companies, managed for value creation, and ultimately returned through exits. Understanding the fund structure is foundational to understanding how PE firms operate, how they make money, and why they behave the way they do.

## The Partnership Structure

Every PE fund is structured as a **limited partnership**. This legal form has two classes of participants:

- **General partners (GPs)** — the PE firm and its principals. The GP manages all aspects of the fund: raising capital, selecting investments, managing portfolio companies, and executing exits. The GP typically contributes 1–2% of total fund capital from its own balance sheet (the "GP commit"), demonstrating alignment with LPs.

- **Limited partners (LPs)** — the investors who provide 98–99% of the capital. LPs are passive: they commit capital but have no role in investment decisions or day-to-day fund management. Their liability is limited to their committed capital — hence "limited partners."

This separation is deliberate. LPs benefit from professional investment management without being exposed to the operational and legal liabilities of running a fund.

## The Fee Structure: "Two and Twenty"

The standard PE fee structure — often called "**two and twenty**" — has two components:

**Management fee:** Typically 2% of committed capital per year during the investment period (often dropping to 1–1.5% of invested capital thereafter). This covers the firm's operating costs: salaries, offices, deal sourcing expenses, and due diligence costs. For a $1 billion fund, that is $20 million per year — substantial, but modest relative to the potential **carried interest** payout.

**Carried interest:** The GP's share of fund profits — typically 20% of returns above a **hurdle rate** (often 8% per year). Carried interest is the primary economic incentive for GP principals and the mechanism that aligns GP and LP interests. If the fund generates strong returns, GPs receive a disproportionately large share of upside. If the fund underperforms its hurdle, the GP earns no carry at all.

> **Carried interest formula:** If a $1 billion fund returns $2 billion after fees, the $1 billion profit is split approximately 80/20 between LPs and the GP — LPs receive $800 million, GP receives $200 million in carried interest.

## The Fund Lifecycle

A typical PE fund progresses through three distinct phases:

### Phase 1: Fundraising and Commitments (Years 0–1)

The GP markets the fund to institutional investors and collects **capital commitments** — pledges to provide capital when called. LPs do not hand over money upfront; they commit to contributing when the GP issues a **capital call** to fund a specific investment.

A typical mid-market fund might raise $500 million to $2 billion in commitments from 20–50 LP investors. Mega-funds raise $10 billion or more.

### Phase 2: Investment Period (Years 1–5)

During the investment period, the GP actively sources, evaluates, and acquires companies. Each acquisition triggers a capital call — the GP sends notice to LPs, who must transfer their pro-rata share of the investment within 10–14 business days.

The investment period typically closes after 5 years or when commitments are substantially deployed, whichever comes first. After this, no new platform investments can be made (though follow-on investments in existing portfolio companies may continue).

**Dry powder** — undeployed committed capital — gives PE firms the flexibility to move quickly on opportunities without needing to raise new capital for each deal.

### Phase 3: Value Creation and Exit (Years 5–10)

Once a company is acquired, the GP works with management to execute the value creation plan: growing revenues, improving margins, making bolt-on acquisitions, or transforming the operating model. The typical hold period is 3–7 years.

Exits return capital to LPs and realise carried interest for the GP. Common exit routes include:

- **Trade sale** — selling to a strategic acquirer (another corporation in the same industry)
- **Secondary buyout** — selling to another PE firm
- **IPO** — listing the portfolio company on a public stock exchange
- **Recapitalisation** — refinancing debt to extract a dividend, partially returning capital while retaining ownership

## The J-Curve Effect

One important feature of PE fund returns is the **J-curve** — the typical pattern where early years show negative or flat returns (as fees are paid and investments are made at cost), followed by strong positive returns in later years as portfolio companies mature and exits are realised.

LPs evaluating PE fund performance in years 1–3 often see disappointing numbers. This is expected and does not indicate poor fund management — it reflects the natural timing of value creation and realisation in private markets.

## Fund Size and Strategy

**Fund size** is a critical strategic variable. Small funds ($100–500 million) focus on the lower mid-market, acquiring businesses with $5–50 million of EBITDA. Large and mega-funds target businesses generating $100 million or more in EBITDA and can pursue transformational platform acquisitions.

Fund size also constrains strategy: a $10 billion fund cannot generate meaningful returns from a $20 million deal. The deployment imperative drives large funds toward large targets.

## Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Limited partner (LP)** — passive investor in a PE fund; provides capital but has no management role
- **General partner (GP)** — the PE firm that manages the fund and makes all investment decisions
- **Management fee** — annual fee paid to the GP to cover fund operating costs; typically 2% of committed capital
- **Carried interest** — the GP's share of fund profits above the hurdle rate; typically 20%; the primary GP incentive
- **Fund lifecycle** — the progression from fundraising through investment, value creation, and exit; typically 10 years
- **Capital call** — a GP notice requiring LPs to contribute their committed capital to fund a specific investment
- **Dry powder** — committed but undeployed LP capital available for future investments
