Every PE deal involves multiple negotiations: with the seller on price and terms, with lenders on debt structure, with management on equity incentives, and with LPs on fund terms. Understanding structured negotiation is a core professional skill.

### BATNA: The Foundation

**BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement)** is the single most important concept in negotiation. Your BATNA is what you will do if this deal does not happen. A strong BATNA (you have other attractive deals in the pipeline) gives you leverage to walk away. A weak BATNA (you need this deal to deploy capital) weakens your position.

Before entering any negotiation, define:
- Your BATNA (what you will do if you walk away)
- Their BATNA (what the seller will do if you walk away — do they have other bidders?)
- Your reservation price (the worst deal you would accept)
- Your target price (the deal you want)

### ZOPA: Where Deals Happen

The **ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement)** is the range between the buyer's maximum price and the seller's minimum price. If your maximum is €250M and the seller's minimum is €220M, the ZOPA is €220–250M. If your maximum is €230M and the seller's minimum is €250M, there is no ZOPA and no deal is possible without changing the terms (e.g., adding an earn-out to bridge the gap).

### PE-Specific Negotiation Dynamics

**Information asymmetry.** The seller knows the business better than the buyer. Due diligence reduces but never eliminates this gap. The seller has incentives to present optimistic information; the buyer must extract reality.

**Competitive tension.** In an auction, the seller uses competing bidders to drive up price. The buyer must balance competitiveness (bidding enough to stay in the process) with discipline (not overbidding).

**Multi-party negotiation.** A single PE deal involves parallel negotiations: with the seller (price, SPA terms), with lenders (debt terms, covenants), with management (equity incentives, employment terms), and sometimes with regulators (competition clearance conditions). Concessions in one negotiation can affect leverage in others.
