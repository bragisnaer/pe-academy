The **Sale and Purchase Agreement (SPA)** is the definitive legal document governing the acquisition. It allocates risk between buyer and seller through a framework of representations, warranties, indemnities, and conditions.

### Core SPA Components

**Purchase price and payment mechanism.** The headline price, the currency, and whether payment is on a "locked box" or "completion accounts" basis.

**Locked box vs. completion accounts:**

> **Market-specific note:** This distinction is significant. European PE deals predominantly use locked box mechanisms. US deals predominantly use completion accounts.

**Locked box (dominant in Europe, Nordics, and UK):** The price is fixed at signing, based on a balance sheet at a recent historical date (the "locked box date"). No post-closing price adjustment. The seller guarantees that no "leakage" (unauthorised extraction of value — dividends, management fees, intercompany transfers) has occurred between the locked box date and closing. This provides price certainty for both parties but requires the buyer to trust that the locked box balance sheet is accurate.

**Completion accounts (dominant in US):** The price is preliminary at signing and adjusted post-closing based on actual working capital, net debt, and cash at the closing date. A completion accounts mechanism typically takes 60–90 days after closing to finalise. This provides greater accuracy but introduces post-closing negotiation and potential disputes.

### Representations and Warranties

**Representations (reps)** are statements of fact made by the seller about the target business at signing and closing. **Warranties** are promises that these statements are true. Common reps and warranties include:

- The financial statements fairly present the company's financial position
- There is no undisclosed litigation
- The company complies with all applicable laws and regulations
- All material contracts are valid and in force
- The company owns or has valid licences for all intellectual property
- There are no undisclosed liabilities
- Tax returns have been filed correctly

If a rep proves untrue after closing, the buyer may have a claim for damages against the seller.

### Indemnities

**Indemnities** are specific promises by the seller to compensate the buyer for identified risks — typically risks discovered during due diligence that are too significant to ignore but insufficient to kill the deal. Common indemnities:

- Tax indemnity (the seller indemnifies the buyer against pre-closing tax liabilities)
- Environmental indemnity (the seller indemnifies against contamination discovered pre-closing)
- Specific litigation indemnity (the seller covers the outcome of a pending lawsuit)

Indemnities provide "pound-for-pound" compensation (the buyer receives the actual loss), unlike warranty claims which may be subject to disclosure limitations.

### Warranty and Indemnity (W&I) Insurance

> **Market-specific note:** W&I insurance has become standard in European (including Nordic) and UK PE deals. It is also growing rapidly in the US but is less universal.

**W&I insurance** is a policy purchased by the buyer (or sometimes the seller) that covers losses arising from breaches of representations and warranties in the SPA. The insurer replaces the seller as the primary source of recovery.

Benefits:
- The buyer has a creditworthy counterparty (the insurer) rather than relying on the seller's covenant
- The seller achieves a "clean exit" with limited ongoing liability (typically limited to fraud)
- Facilitates smoother negotiations because warranty scope is less contentious when backed by insurance
- Standard in competitive auction processes where sellers demand limited post-closing liability

W&I premiums are typically 1–3% of the insured limit. The policy is subject to a retention (deductible), typically 0.5–1% of enterprise value.
