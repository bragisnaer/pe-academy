### Key Terms in Debt Negotiation

**Pricing (interest rate).** Expressed as a spread over a reference rate (SOFR in the US, EURIBOR in Europe, SONIA in the UK) plus a fixed margin. Example: EURIBOR + 450 basis points. The margin reflects the borrower's credit risk.

**Leverage covenant.** Maximum permitted leverage ratio (Debt/EBITDA). Typically tested quarterly. Breach triggers default and renegotiation.

**Interest coverage covenant.** Minimum permitted ratio of EBITDA to interest expense. Protects the lender by ensuring the company generates enough cash to pay interest.

**Amortisation.** Mandatory scheduled repayment of principal. Term loan A (TLA) amortises on a schedule (e.g., 5% per year). Term loan B (TLB) is typically "bullet" repayment (minimal amortisation, full principal repaid at maturity). TLB is dominant in the leveraged loan market.

**Prepayment terms.** Can the borrower repay early without penalty? Soft call provisions (e.g., 101% repayment in year 1, par thereafter) and non-call periods (cannot repay during the first 1–2 years) affect the borrower's flexibility.

**Cash sweep.** Mandatory prepayment from excess cash flow (typically 50–75%). The lender captures surplus cash to accelerate deleveraging.

**Permitted acquisitions.** Conditions under which the company can make bolt-on acquisitions without lender consent. Typically limited by size (e.g., any single acquisition below 10% of EBITDA) and leverage test (pro forma leverage after the acquisition must be below a specified threshold).

**Dividend restriction.** Limitations on the company's ability to pay dividends to the PE fund. Typically restricted unless leverage is below a specified level (e.g., dividends permitted only if pro forma leverage is below 3.5x).

### Negotiation Strategy

Buyer priorities: maximum flexibility (loose covenants, no amortisation, broad permitted baskets), lowest cost, and certainty of funding.

Lender priorities: tight covenants (early warning of distress), amortisation (gradual risk reduction), restrictive baskets (limiting the borrower's ability to take actions that increase risk), and adequate return for risk.

The negotiation outcome depends on market conditions. In a loose credit market, borrowers extract more flexibility. In a tight market, lenders impose stricter terms.
