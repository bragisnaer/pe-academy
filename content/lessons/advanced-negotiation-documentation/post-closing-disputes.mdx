### Completion Accounts Disputes

In completion accounts deals, the buyer prepares draft closing accounts (working capital, net debt, cash) within 60–90 days of closing. The seller reviews and may dispute individual line items. Common areas of dispute:

- Whether specific accruals are operating (included in working capital) or non-operating (excluded)
- Treatment of intercompany balances
- Classification of cash as "free" or "restricted"
- Whether certain payables are operating (included in working capital) or debt-like (deducted from equity value)

If the parties cannot agree, the dispute is typically referred to an independent accountant (often a Big Four firm not involved in the transaction) for binding determination.

### Warranty Claims

If the buyer discovers a breach of warranty after closing (e.g., undisclosed litigation, inaccurate financial statements), it may bring a warranty claim against the seller (or the W&I insurer). Key limitations:

- **Time limits:** Warranty claims must typically be brought within 18–24 months of closing (except for tax and fundamental warranties, which may have longer survival periods)
- **De minimis threshold:** Individual claims below a specified amount (e.g., €50,000) are excluded
- **Basket (deductible):** Claims are only payable once aggregate claims exceed a specified threshold (typically 0.5–1% of enterprise value)
- **Cap:** Total seller liability is capped (typically 10–30% of enterprise value for non-fundamental warranties; 100% for fundamental warranties like title and authority)

### Earn-Out Disputes

Earn-out disputes are among the most common post-closing conflicts. The buyer controls the business and may make operational decisions (cost cuts, strategic pivots, pricing changes) that reduce the earn-out metric. The seller argues the buyer deliberately suppressed the earn-out. Clear drafting of the earn-out methodology — including specific definitions of what costs can and cannot be allocated — is the best prevention.

### Key Terms Introduced in This Module

- **BATNA** — Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement; your fallback if the deal fails
- **ZOPA** — Zone of Possible Agreement; the range where a deal is possible
- **Locked box** — a pricing mechanism where the price is fixed at signing based on a historical balance sheet
- **Completion accounts** — a pricing mechanism where the price is adjusted post-closing based on actual closing-date financials
- **W&I insurance** — warranty and indemnity insurance; a policy covering losses from SPA warranty breaches
- **Ratchet** — a mechanism increasing management's equity percentage if the PE fund achieves a specified return threshold
- **Tag-along / drag-along** — rights ensuring management can (tag) or must (drag) sell alongside the PE fund at exit
