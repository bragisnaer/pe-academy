This lesson provides a snapshot of the PE industry as it stands, covering market size, regional dynamics, and the competitive landscape. Understanding the current state of the market gives context to every subsequent lesson.

### Global Market Size

The global private equity industry manages over $8 trillion in assets. Buyout funds represent the largest single strategy within that figure, but growth equity, private credit, infrastructure, and secondaries have all grown rapidly.

Annual PE deal volume globally has exceeded $1 trillion in peak years (2021 was a record), though it contracted significantly in 2022–2023 as interest rates rose and credit markets tightened. Activity began recovering in 2024 as rate expectations stabilised.

### Regional Distribution

**North America** remains the largest PE market by deal volume and fund size, accounting for roughly 55–60% of global PE AUM. The US has the deepest capital markets, the most developed LP base, and the highest concentration of mega-funds.

**Europe** is the second-largest market, accounting for approximately 25–30% of global PE activity. The UK is the largest European market, followed by Germany, France, and the Nordics. European PE has distinct characteristics:
- Greater prevalence of family-owned businesses as acquisition targets
- Stronger regulatory overlay (AIFMD, GDPR, sustainability reporting)
- More conservative leverage levels than US deals on average
- Deep mid-market activity, with many specialist firms focused on businesses with €10–100 million EBITDA

The **Nordic PE market** punches above its weight relative to population size. Firms like EQT, Nordic Capital, Altor, IK Partners, and Hg Capital have built strong track records, and the region produces a disproportionate number of technology and healthcare deals.

**Asia-Pacific** has grown rapidly, with China, India, Japan, South Korea, and Southeast Asia all attracting increasing PE capital. The region accounts for roughly 15–20% of global PE activity. Regulatory environments, exit markets, and governance standards vary significantly across Asian jurisdictions.

**Middle East and Africa** represent smaller but growing PE markets. Sovereign wealth funds from the Gulf states (ADIA, Mubadala, PIF) are major LPs globally while also investing directly in their home regions.

### The Competitive Landscape

The PE industry operates at multiple tiers:

**Mega-funds** (AUM $50 billion+): Blackstone, KKR, Apollo, Carlyle, TPG, CVC, EQT, Thoma Bravo, Advent International, Warburg Pincus. These firms compete for the largest deals, manage diversified strategies, and have deep relationships with the largest institutional LPs.

**Upper mid-market** (AUM $5–50 billion): Firms like Hg Capital, Cinven, PAI Partners, Bridgepoint, Genstar Capital, GTCR, Hellman & Friedman. Many are sector-specialists with concentrated portfolios and strong operational capabilities.

**Mid-market** (AUM $1–5 billion): Hundreds of firms globally, often focused on specific sectors or geographies. Mid-market PE is where many of the highest-returning funds operate, because smaller deals attract less competition and offer more operational improvement potential.

**Lower mid-market and small buyout** (AUM under $1 billion): Firms acquiring businesses with $3–30 million EBITDA. Often focused on founder-owned businesses, family successions, or corporate carve-outs. Relationship-driven sourcing is critical at this level.

### Key Trends Shaping the Industry

**1. The rise of private credit.** Banks have retreated from leveraged lending since the financial crisis. Private credit funds (Ares, Blue Owl, HPS, Golub Capital) have filled the gap, providing unitranche loans directly to PE-backed companies. The global private credit market now exceeds $1.5 trillion in AUM.

**2. Technology sector dominance.** Software and technology businesses now represent the single largest sector for PE deal activity, driven by recurring revenue models, high margins, and scalable operations. Firms like Thoma Bravo and Vista Equity Partners have built entire platforms around software buyouts.

**3. Longer hold periods and continuation vehicles.** The average PE hold period has extended from roughly 4 years in 2005 to over 5.5 years today. GP-led continuation vehicles — where a GP moves a portfolio company from an older fund into a new vehicle — have become a common mechanism to extend ownership of high-performing assets.

**4. LP consolidation.** Large LPs are concentrating their PE allocations with fewer GP relationships, often demanding co-investment rights, lower fees, and greater transparency in exchange for larger commitments. This "flight to scale" favours established firms with proven track records.

**5. Regulatory evolution.** The SEC in the US and ESMA/national regulators in Europe continue to increase scrutiny of PE fund practices, particularly around fee transparency, valuation methodology, and conflicts of interest.

### Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Mid-market** — the segment of PE focused on businesses with approximately $10–100 million EBITDA; often where the highest fund returns are generated
- **Private credit** — direct lending by non-bank funds to companies, bypassing traditional bank financing
- **Continuation vehicle** — a GP-led secondary transaction that moves a portfolio company from an existing fund into a new fund, extending the hold period
- **Co-investment** — the right for an LP to invest directly alongside the GP in a specific deal, typically on reduced or zero-fee terms
