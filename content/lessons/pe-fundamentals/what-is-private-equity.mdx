**Private equity** is a form of alternative investment where funds pool capital from institutional and accredited investors to acquire ownership stakes in private companies — those not listed or traded on public stock exchanges.

Unlike buying shares in Apple or Microsoft through a brokerage account, private equity involves directly owning and actively managing businesses over a multi-year investment horizon, with the goal of improving their performance and ultimately selling them at a profit.

## What Makes Private Equity Different from Public Markets?

Public equity investors buy and sell shares continuously on stock exchanges. They have limited influence over the businesses they own and rely primarily on dividends and share price appreciation for returns.

**Private equity** operates entirely differently:

- Investments are illiquid — capital is locked up for years, not days
- Ownership stakes are concentrated — PE firms typically own 50–100% of a **portfolio company**
- Management is active — PE firms work closely with company leadership to drive operational improvement
- Returns come primarily from the exit — when the PE firm sells the business, either to another buyer or via an IPO

This illiquidity premium is a core reason why PE has historically delivered higher returns than public markets — investors demand compensation for locking up their capital for 5–10 years.

## The Basic Value Creation Thesis

Every private equity investment rests on a fundamental logic: **buy a business, improve it, sell it for more than you paid**.

The improvement can take many forms:

- **Revenue growth** — entering new markets, expanding product lines, adding sales resources
- **Margin improvement** — reducing costs, improving procurement, consolidating functions
- **Multiple expansion** — buying at a lower valuation multiple and selling at a higher one, often by improving business quality or timing the market
- **Leverage** — using borrowed money to amplify equity returns (covered in detail in the LBO Basics lesson)

In practice, most successful PE deals combine several of these levers. A PE firm might acquire a regional healthcare business, expand it into three new geographies (revenue growth), centralise back-office functions (margin improvement), and exit when healthcare sector valuations are favourable (multiple expansion).

## The Structure of Private Equity

**Private equity** funds are structured as limited partnerships:

- **General Partners (GPs)** — the PE firm itself. GPs manage the fund, make investment decisions, and typically contribute 1–2% of total fund capital alongside investor capital. GPs earn a management fee (usually 2% of committed capital per year) and **carried interest** (typically 20% of profits) as incentive compensation.
- **Limited Partners (LPs)** — the investors who commit the vast majority of the capital. LPs include pension funds, endowments, sovereign wealth funds, insurance companies, and wealthy family offices. LPs are passive — they commit capital but have no say in which deals are made.

This structure creates a powerful alignment of interests: GPs only earn substantial carried interest if they generate strong returns for LPs.

## Scale and Scope of the Asset Class

Private equity has grown enormously since its emergence in the 1970s and 1980s. Today, PE firms globally manage well over $5 trillion in **assets under management (AUM)**, with the largest firms — Blackstone, KKR, Apollo, Carlyle — each overseeing hundreds of billions.

**Portfolio companies** owned by PE firms collectively employ millions of people and span every industry sector: technology, healthcare, consumer goods, industrials, financial services, and infrastructure.

For anyone working in finance, advising businesses, or operating at senior levels in almost any industry, understanding how private equity works is now a baseline professional requirement. PE buyers are active in virtually every M&A transaction of scale, and the logic of PE valuation permeates corporate finance far beyond the asset class itself.

## Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Private equity** — investment in privately held companies, pooled through a fund structure
- **Portfolio company** — a business owned by a PE fund as part of its investment portfolio
- **General partner (GP)** — the PE firm that manages the fund and makes investment decisions
- **Limited partner (LP)** — an investor in a PE fund who provides capital but takes no active management role
- **Carried interest** — the GP's share of fund profits, typically 20% above a hurdle rate
- **Assets under management (AUM)** — total capital a firm manages on behalf of investors
- **Value creation** — the strategies PE firms use to increase a portfolio company's value during the ownership period
