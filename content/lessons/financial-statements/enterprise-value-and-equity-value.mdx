Understanding the distinction between **enterprise value (EV)** and **equity value** is essential to PE deal structuring, valuation, and return analysis.

### Enterprise Value

**Enterprise value** represents the total value of a business, attributable to all capital providers — both debt holders and equity holders.

> **EV = Equity Value + Net Debt**

Where Net Debt = Total Debt – Cash and Cash Equivalents.

EV is the price to acquire the entire business, inclusive of all obligations. When PE firms quote acquisition multiples (eV/EBITDA), they use enterprise value because it is capital-structure-neutral — it measures business value regardless of how the business is financed.

### Equity Value

**Equity value** represents the value attributable only to equity holders — what remains after all debts are paid.

> **Equity Value = Enterprise Value – Net Debt**

In an LBO, the PE firm's equity investment represents the equity value at entry. At exit, the equity value equals the enterprise value at exit minus any remaining debt. The difference between entry and exit equity value (plus any interim distributions) determines the PE fund's return.

### The Bridge Between EV and Equity Value

In practice, the "bridge" from enterprise value to equity value involves more than just debt and cash. A complete bridge includes:

- Total financial debt (bank loans, bonds, shareholder loans)
- Cash and cash equivalents (subtracted)
- Pension deficit (added to EV)
- Capital lease obligations (added to EV)
- Minority interests (added to EV if consolidated subsidiaries have outside shareholders)
- Preferred equity (added to EV)
- Working capital adjustments (to the extent actual working capital at closing differs from the normalised level assumed in the deal price)

Getting the EV-to-equity bridge right is critical in deal negotiations because small differences in bridge items can shift millions between buyer and seller.

### Why This Matters in LBO Analysis

Consider a simplified example:

- A company has an enterprise value of $500 million (10x EBITDA of $50 million)
- At entry, the PE firm finances the deal with $175 million equity and $325 million debt
- Over 5 years, EBITDA grows to $65 million and the exit multiple is 11x
- Exit EV = $65M x 11 = $715 million
- Remaining debt at exit = $225 million (after $100 million of debt paydown)
- Exit equity value = $715M – $225M = $490 million

The equity value grew from $175 million to $490 million — a 2.8x MOIC. This return was driven by all three LBO return drivers: EBITDA growth ($50M to $65M), multiple expansion (10x to 11x), and debt paydown ($325M to $225M).

### Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Enterprise value (EV)** — the total value of a business, including both debt and equity claims; the standard acquisition price metric
- **Equity value** — the residual value attributable to equity holders after all debts are paid
- **Net debt** — total financial debt minus cash and cash equivalents
- **EV bridge** — the detailed reconciliation of items that convert enterprise value to equity value (debt, cash, pensions, minorities, working capital adjustments)
