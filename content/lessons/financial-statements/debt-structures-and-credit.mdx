Debt is the engine of the leveraged buyout. Understanding debt structures, credit terminology, and lender behaviour is fundamental to PE analysis.

### Types of LBO Debt

**Senior secured debt** — the most senior claim on a company's assets. In the event of default, senior secured lenders are repaid first. Provided by banks (syndicated broadly) or by direct lenders (held on a single fund's balance sheet). Features the lowest interest rate but the most restrictive covenants.

**Unitranche debt** — a single loan facility that combines senior and subordinated debt into one instrument, provided by a single direct lender or lender group. Unitranche has become the dominant financing structure for mid-market LBOs in both the US and Europe because it is faster to arrange, simpler to negotiate, and provides certainty of funding. The interest rate is higher than pure senior debt but lower than a combination of senior plus mezzanine.

**Second lien debt** — secured debt with a subordinated claim on assets. If the company defaults, second lien lenders are repaid only after senior secured lenders are fully satisfied. Higher interest rate to compensate for the additional risk.

**Mezzanine debt** — unsecured subordinated debt that sits between senior debt and equity in the capital structure. Mezzanine carries the highest interest rate of any debt tranche and often includes **equity warrants** (the right to purchase company equity at a predetermined price), giving mezzanine lenders potential upside participation.

**High-yield bonds** — below-investment-grade bonds issued in public or private markets. Used primarily in large-cap LBOs. High-yield bonds are typically unsecured, have longer maturities (7–10 years), and may be callable (the issuer can repay early) after a specified non-call period.

**Payment-in-kind (PIK) debt** — debt where interest is not paid in cash but added to the principal balance. PIK preserves cash flow for the company but increases total debt over time. Often used in highly leveraged or stressed situations.

### Key Credit Metrics

PE firms and lenders monitor several ratios to assess a company's ability to service its debt:

- **Leverage ratio (Total Debt / EBITDA)** — the headline metric. A 5x leverage ratio means the company has 5 years of EBITDA in debt.
- **Interest coverage ratio (EBITDA / Interest Expense)** — measures the company's ability to pay interest from operating earnings. A ratio of 2.0x or higher is typically the minimum threshold for comfort.
- **Fixed charge coverage ratio (EBITDA / (Interest + Mandatory Amortisation))** — a more conservative measure that includes required principal repayments, not just interest.
- **Debt service coverage ratio (FCF / Total Debt Service)** — measures whether the company generates enough free cash flow to cover all debt obligations (interest plus amortisation).
- **Net debt / EBITDA** — adjusts the leverage ratio for cash on hand.

### Covenants

Debt agreements include **covenants** — contractual restrictions designed to protect lenders:

**Maintenance covenants** require the company to meet specified financial ratios (typically tested quarterly). If the company breaches a maintenance covenant, it is in technical default. Traditional bank loans use maintenance covenants.

**Incurrence covenants** only restrict the company when it takes a specific action (e.g., incurring additional debt, making an acquisition, paying a dividend). The company can have weak financial performance without breaching incurrence covenants as long as it does not trigger the restricted actions. High-yield bonds and many direct lending facilities use incurrence covenants.

**Covenant-lite (cov-lite) loans** contain incurrence covenants rather than maintenance covenants, giving borrowers more flexibility. Cov-lite has become the standard in large-cap leveraged loans — a development that concerns credit analysts because it reduces early warning signals of distress.

### Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Unitranche** — a single debt facility combining senior and subordinated debt; the dominant mid-market LBO financing structure
- **Covenant** — a contractual restriction in a debt agreement designed to protect lenders
- **Maintenance covenant** — a financial test that must be met at regular intervals (typically quarterly)
- **Incurrence covenant** — a restriction triggered only when the borrower takes a specific action
- **PIK (Payment-in-Kind)** — debt where interest accrues to the principal rather than being paid in cash
- **Interest coverage ratio** — EBITDA divided by interest expense; a measure of debt serviceability
