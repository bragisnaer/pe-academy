Working capital is where accounting meets operations. It measures the cash tied up in the day-to-day cycle of buying inputs, producing goods or services, and collecting payment from customers. In PE, working capital analysis is critical for two reasons: it affects how much cash a business truly generates, and it is a common source of value creation through optimisation.

### The Working Capital Cycle

**Net working capital (NWC)** = Current operating assets – Current operating liabilities

The main components:

- **Accounts receivable (AR)** — money owed by customers. Measured in "days sales outstanding" (DSO): DSO = (AR / Revenue) x 365. A DSO of 45 means customers take 45 days on average to pay.
- **Inventory** — goods held for sale. Measured in "days inventory outstanding" (DIO): DIO = (Inventory / COGS) x 365.
- **Accounts payable (AP)** — money owed to suppliers. Measured in "days payable outstanding" (DPO): DPO = (AP / COGS) x 365.

**The cash conversion cycle (CCC)** = DSO + DIO – DPO. This measures how many days the company must fund its operations from cash before collecting payment.

A company with DSO of 45, DIO of 30, and DPO of 60 has a CCC of 15 days — relatively efficient. A company with DSO of 90, DIO of 60, and DPO of 30 has a CCC of 120 days — it must fund 120 days of operations from cash before it collects.

### Working Capital in Deal Pricing

In virtually all PE transactions, the purchase price assumes a **normalised working capital** level. The SPA includes a working capital adjustment mechanism:

- If actual working capital at closing exceeds the agreed normal level, the buyer pays the seller additional consideration (the seller left more value in the business)
- If actual working capital at closing is below the agreed normal level, the purchase price is reduced (the seller stripped cash from the business before closing)

Working capital negotiations are among the most contentious aspects of deal completion. Differences of a few million can arise from disagreements about what constitutes "normal" seasonal levels, whether certain accruals are operating or non-operating, and how to treat unusual items.

### Working Capital as a Value Creation Lever

PE firms frequently improve working capital efficiency post-acquisition:

- **Reducing DSO** — tightening credit terms, improving invoicing processes, incentivising early payment
- **Reducing DIO** — improving demand forecasting, reducing safety stock, implementing just-in-time practices
- **Extending DPO** — negotiating longer payment terms with suppliers (though this can damage supplier relationships if pushed too aggressively)

Every day removed from the cash conversion cycle releases cash. For a business with $500 million in annual revenue, reducing DSO by 5 days frees approximately $6.8 million in cash.

### Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Net working capital (NWC)** — current operating assets minus current operating liabilities
- **DSO (Days Sales Outstanding)** — the average number of days to collect payment from customers
- **DIO (Days Inventory Outstanding)** — the average number of days inventory is held before sale
- **DPO (Days Payable Outstanding)** — the average number of days to pay suppliers
- **Cash conversion cycle (CCC)** — DSO + DIO – DPO; the number of days cash is tied up in operations
- **Working capital adjustment** — the mechanism in an SPA that adjusts the purchase price based on the difference between actual and normalised working capital at closing
