Free cash flow is the ultimate test of a business's financial health. It is the cash a business generates after funding all operating costs and necessary capital investment — the cash available to service debt, fund acquisitions, or return to equity holders.

### The FCF Calculation

> **Free Cash Flow = EBITDA – Cash Taxes – Changes in Working Capital – Capital Expenditure**

Or equivalently:

> **Free Cash Flow = Cash from Operations – Capital Expenditure**

### FCF Yield and Conversion

**FCF yield** = Free Cash Flow / Enterprise Value. This measures how much cash the business generates relative to its total value. A 10% FCF yield means the business generates cash equal to 10% of its enterprise value each year. High FCF yield businesses are attractive LBO candidates because the cash can service debt rapidly.

**FCF conversion** = Free Cash Flow / EBITDA. This ratio shows how much of EBITDA actually converts to cash. A business with 80% FCF conversion is efficient; one with 40% conversion may have high capex requirements, working capital intensity, or non-cash EBITDA adjustments.

> **Rule of thumb:** A strong LBO candidate typically has FCF conversion above 60–70%. Below 50% signals that EBITDA overstates the business's true cash generation.

### Unlevered vs. Levered FCF

**Unlevered free cash flow (UFCF)** is calculated before interest and debt repayments — it represents the cash available to all capital providers (debt and equity). This is the cash flow used in DCF valuations.

**Levered free cash flow (LFCF)** is calculated after interest and mandatory debt repayments — it represents the cash available only to equity holders. In LBO models, levered FCF determines how much cash is available for discretionary debt repayment.

### Exercise: FCF Analysis

**Task:** A company reports the following annual figures:
- Revenue: €200 million
- EBITDA: €40 million (20% margin)
- Cash taxes: €6 million
- Change in working capital: -€3 million (working capital increased, consuming cash)
- Capital expenditure: €12 million

Calculate: (a) Free cash flow, (b) FCF conversion, (c) FCF yield if EV is €400 million.

**Solution:**
- (a) FCF = €40M – €6M – €3M – €12M = **€19 million**
- (b) FCF conversion = €19M / €40M = **47.5%**
- (c) FCF yield = €19M / €400M = **4.75%**

This business has a below-average FCF conversion rate. A PE buyer would investigate whether the capex is unusually high (growth investment?) and whether working capital can be optimised.

### Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Free cash flow (FCF)** — EBITDA minus cash taxes, working capital changes, and capex; the cash available after all operating needs are met
- **FCF yield** — free cash flow divided by enterprise value; a measure of cash generation efficiency relative to business value
- **FCF conversion** — free cash flow divided by EBITDA; measures how much EBITDA translates into actual cash
- **Unlevered free cash flow** — FCF before interest and debt service; the cash available to all capital providers
- **Levered free cash flow** — FCF after interest and mandatory debt repayment; the cash available to equity holders
