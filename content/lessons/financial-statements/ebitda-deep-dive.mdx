EBITDA is the language of private equity. Entry multiples, exit multiples, leverage ratios, and value creation are all measured against EBITDA. A PE professional who does not understand EBITDA deeply — including its variations, its limitations, and how it can be manipulated — is at a fundamental disadvantage.

### What EBITDA Actually Measures

**EBITDA** strips out four categories of expense from net income:

- **Interest** — removed because it reflects the capital structure (which changes in an LBO), not operating performance
- **Taxes** — removed because tax rates vary by jurisdiction and structure, and PE firms optimise tax through structuring
- **Depreciation** — a non-cash charge reflecting the decline in value of physical assets over their useful life
- **Amortisation** — a non-cash charge reflecting the decline in value of intangible assets (often from prior acquisitions)

The result is a metric that approximates operating cash generation. Approximates — not equals. EBITDA is not free cash flow. It ignores capex, working capital changes, and cash taxes.

### Reported EBITDA vs. Adjusted EBITDA

Sellers almost always present **adjusted EBITDA** — a figure that adds back one-time, non-recurring, or non-cash items to make the business look more profitable than reported EBITDA suggests.

Common add-backs include:

- Restructuring costs (redundancy payments, facility closures)
- Litigation settlements
- Transaction-related expenses (advisory fees, legal costs from prior deals)
- Share-based compensation
- Non-recurring professional fees
- Management fees paid to a prior PE owner
- Above-market owner compensation in founder-led businesses

**The critical question for every add-back: is this item genuinely non-recurring, or is it a recurring cost presented as one-time?**

A company that books "restructuring costs" every year for five consecutive years does not have non-recurring restructuring costs. It has a recurring operational problem. Experienced PE investors scrutinise every add-back during financial due diligence.

### Run-Rate EBITDA

**Run-rate EBITDA** goes further than adjusted EBITDA by incorporating the full-year effect of recent changes:

- If a new contract was signed in October, run-rate EBITDA includes 12 months of that contract's revenue and margin
- If a cost reduction programme was completed in Q3, run-rate EBITDA reflects the full annualised savings
- If a bolt-on acquisition closed in July, run-rate EBITDA includes the full-year contribution

Run-rate EBITDA is inherently forward-looking and subjective. It is commonly used in sale processes to present the most favourable view of the business. Buyers must validate each run-rate assumption during due diligence.

### EBITDA Margins

**EBITDA margin** = EBITDA / Revenue. This ratio measures operating efficiency and is a primary benchmarking tool:

- Software businesses: 30–50% EBITDA margins are common
- Healthcare services: 15–25%
- Industrial manufacturing: 10–20%
- Retail: 5–15%

Margin trends matter more than a single snapshot. Expanding margins suggest improving efficiency or pricing power. Declining margins signal competitive pressure, cost inflation, or operational issues.

### EBITDA Limitations

PE professionals must understand what EBITDA does NOT capture:

- **Capex requirements** — a business with 25% EBITDA margins but 15% capex-to-revenue is generating only 10% in true free cash flow margin
- **Working capital needs** — growing businesses often require increasing working capital investment, consuming cash that EBITDA implies is available
- **Cash taxes** — EBITDA ignores taxes, but taxes are a real cash outflow
- **Lease payments** — under older accounting standards, operating leases were excluded from EBITDA; IFRS 16 has largely addressed this, but the treatment varies
- **Stock-based compensation** — often added back to adjusted EBITDA, but it represents real economic cost to equity holders through dilution

### Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Adjusted EBITDA** — EBITDA with add-backs for non-recurring, non-cash, or non-operational items
- **Run-rate EBITDA** — adjusted EBITDA further adjusted for the full-year effect of recent changes (new contracts, cost savings, acquisitions)
- **EBITDA margin** — EBITDA divided by revenue; a measure of operating profitability
- **Add-back** — an item added to reported EBITDA to arrive at adjusted or normalised EBITDA
- **Quality of earnings** — the analysis that validates whether reported or adjusted EBITDA represents true, sustainable, recurring profitability
