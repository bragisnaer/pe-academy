Every PE deal starts with numbers. Before a firm builds an LBO model or negotiates a purchase price, it must understand the target company's financial reality. The three core financial statements — income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement — provide that foundation.

PE professionals read financial statements differently from auditors or regulators. The objective is not compliance. It is to understand the economic reality of a business: how it earns money, where the cash goes, and what the true underlying profitability looks like once noise is stripped away.

### The Income Statement (Profit & Loss)

The income statement shows revenue, costs, and profit over a period. For PE analysis, the critical line items are:

**Revenue** — the top line. PE investors examine revenue quality: Is it recurring (subscriptions, long-term contracts) or transactional (one-time purchases)? Is it growing? Is it concentrated in a few customers, or diversified?

**Gross profit and gross margin** — revenue minus cost of goods sold (COGS). Gross margin reveals pricing power and production efficiency. A business with 70% gross margins has far more room to absorb cost increases or pricing pressure than one with 25% gross margins.

**EBITDA** — the central profitability metric in PE. Calculated as operating profit plus depreciation and amortisation (or revenue minus all operating costs before interest, tax, D&A). EBITDA approximates operating cash generation before capital structure effects. PE firms live in EBITDA.

**Net income** — the bottom line, after interest, taxes, and all non-cash charges. Less useful in PE analysis because it includes capital structure effects (interest expense) that will change post-acquisition, and non-cash accounting items (amortisation of acquired intangibles) that do not reflect operating performance.

### The Balance Sheet

The balance sheet is a snapshot of what a company owns (assets), owes (liabilities), and the residual claim of owners (equity) at a point in time.

For PE investors, the key focus areas are:

**Working capital** — current assets (receivables, inventory) minus current liabilities (payables, accrued expenses). Working capital ties up cash. A business that needs to fund 90 days of inventory and offers 60-day payment terms to customers requires significant working capital investment to grow. PE firms analyse working capital intensity (working capital as a percentage of revenue) and look for optimisation opportunities.

**Fixed assets and capex** — property, plant, and equipment (PP&E) plus intangible assets. PE firms prefer asset-light businesses where growth does not require heavy capital investment. High capex requirements reduce free cash flow available for debt service.

**Debt structure** — the types, maturities, and covenants of existing debt. In an LBO, existing debt is typically refinanced with new acquisition financing. Understanding the existing structure informs the refinancing plan.

**Off-balance-sheet items** — operating leases (now largely on-balance-sheet under IFRS 16 / ASC 842), pension obligations, contingent liabilities, and guarantees. These represent real economic obligations that may not be immediately visible.

### The Cash Flow Statement

The cash flow statement is the most important financial statement for PE analysis, because PE returns are driven by cash, not accounting profits.

The statement has three sections:

**Cash from operations** — net income adjusted for non-cash items (depreciation, amortisation, share-based compensation) and changes in working capital. This is the cash the business generates from its core activities.

**Cash from investing** — capital expenditure (capex), acquisitions, and asset disposals. The key figure is **maintenance capex** (the minimum investment required to sustain current operations) versus **growth capex** (investment to expand capacity or enter new markets).

**Cash from financing** — debt issuance and repayment, equity transactions, and dividends. In an LBO context, this section reflects the debt service and repayment schedule.

**Free cash flow (FCF)** — the amount of cash remaining after operating expenses and capital expenditure. FCF = Cash from Operations minus Capital Expenditure. This is the cash available to service debt, fund acquisitions, or distribute to equity holders. In LBO analysis, FCF is the single most critical metric because it determines how quickly acquisition debt can be repaid.

### Financial Statement Adjustments in PE Context

PE firms do not take reported financials at face value. Standard adjustments include:

- **Normalisation** — removing one-time items (restructuring costs, litigation settlements, asset write-downs) to reveal underlying recurring profitability
- **Pro forma adjustments** — reflecting the full-year effect of mid-year acquisitions, cost savings already initiated, or run-rate revenue from new contracts
- **Owner adjustments** — in founder-owned businesses, adding back above-market owner compensation, personal expenses run through the business, or related-party transactions at non-market rates
- **Lease adjustments** — ensuring operating leases are properly reflected in enterprise value and EBITDA calculations

These adjustments are the domain of **quality of earnings (QofE)** analysis, covered in depth in Level 4 (Due Diligence).

### Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Free cash flow (FCF)** — cash from operations minus capital expenditure; the cash available for debt service, acquisitions, or distributions
- **Working capital** — current assets minus current liabilities; the cash tied up in day-to-day operations
- **Maintenance capex** — the minimum capital expenditure required to sustain current operations without growth
- **Growth capex** — capital expenditure to expand capacity, enter new markets, or build new capabilities
- **Normalisation** — adjusting reported financials to remove non-recurring items and reveal underlying profitability
