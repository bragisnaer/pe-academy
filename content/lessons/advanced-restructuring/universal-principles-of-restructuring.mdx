### When Restructuring Becomes Necessary

A company enters the restructuring zone when it cannot meet its financial obligations as they come due. This can manifest as:

- **Covenant breach:** The company breaches a leverage ratio or interest coverage covenant, triggering a technical default
- **Liquidity crisis:** The company runs out of cash to pay suppliers, employees, or lenders
- **Maturity wall:** Significant debt maturities are approaching and the company cannot refinance at affordable rates
- **Operational deterioration:** Sustained revenue decline or margin compression that makes the capital structure unsustainable

### The Priority Waterfall

In any insolvency or restructuring, creditor claims are satisfied in order of priority. While the specifics vary by jurisdiction, the general principle is universal:

1. **Secured creditors** (claims secured by specific assets or all assets) — first priority
2. **Priority claims** (employee wages, tax claims, administration costs) — jurisdiction-specific priority ranking
3. **Senior unsecured creditors** — general unsecured claims (trade creditors, unsecured bondholders)
4. **Subordinated creditors** — mezzanine lenders, second lien holders
5. **Equity holders** — last in line; equity receives value only after all creditors are satisfied in full

This is the **absolute priority rule** — senior claims must be satisfied before junior claims receive anything. In practice, deviations from absolute priority occur (particularly in negotiated restructurings) but the principle governs the starting framework.

### The Fulcrum Security

The **fulcrum security** is the tranche of debt that is partially but not fully covered by the company's enterprise value. Creditors above the fulcrum are "in the money" (fully recovered). Creditors below the fulcrum (and equity) receive partial or zero recovery. Identifying the fulcrum security is the starting point for distressed analysis:

**Example:** A company with €300M enterprise value and the following capital structure:
- Senior secured debt: €200M → fully covered (recovery: 100%)
- Second lien debt: €80M → partially covered (€300M – €200M = €100M remaining; €80M out of €100M is covered → recovery: 100%)
- Unsecured bonds: €100M → remaining €20M (€100M – €80M) covers only €20M → recovery: 20%
- Equity: €0 → nothing remaining → wiped out

The fulcrum security is the unsecured bonds — the tranche that crosses the line between full recovery and partial recovery. In a restructuring, the fulcrum security holder typically converts to equity ownership of the restructured company.

### Consensual vs. Formal Restructuring

**Consensual (out-of-court) restructuring:** The company and its creditors negotiate a voluntary agreement — extending maturities, reducing interest rates, converting debt to equity, or injecting new capital. Faster, cheaper, and less disruptive than formal proceedings, but requires creditor consensus.

**Formal (court-supervised) restructuring:** When consensual agreement fails, the company enters a court-supervised process governed by national insolvency law. The court provides a framework for imposing a restructuring plan on dissenting creditors (cramdown).
