> **Market-specific: United Kingdom**

The UK offers several restructuring tools:

### Administration

The most common UK restructuring procedure. An administrator (a licensed insolvency practitioner from a firm like Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG, or Alvarez & Marsal) takes control of the company with the objective of rescuing the business as a going concern, achieving a better result for creditors than liquidation, or distributing proceeds to secured creditors.

**Key differences from Chapter 11:**
- Management loses control (the administrator runs the company)
- No DIP financing equivalent (though the administrator can obtain new facilities with court approval)
- Faster but less flexible than Chapter 11
- Moratorium protects the company from creditor enforcement during administration

### Company Voluntary Arrangement (CVA)

A CVA allows a company to reach a binding agreement with its unsecured creditors to restructure debts (typically paying a percentage over time). The company continues to trade under management control. CVAs have been used by UK retailers (Debenhams, Arcadia Group) to reduce rent obligations, though landlords have criticised the process.

### Restructuring Plan (Part 26A, Companies Act 2006)

Introduced in 2020, the UK Restructuring Plan is the closest UK equivalent to Chapter 11 cramdown. It allows a company to propose a restructuring plan that binds all creditors, including dissenting classes, if the court is satisfied the plan is fair and that dissenting creditors would not be worse off than in the relevant alternative (typically administration or liquidation).

### Pre-Pack Administration

A pre-pack involves arranging a sale of the company's business and assets before the administration is formally commenced. The sale completes immediately upon the administrator's appointment. Pre-packs are controversial because they can leave unsecured creditors with minimal recovery while the business continues under new ownership (sometimes the same management team or PE sponsor).
