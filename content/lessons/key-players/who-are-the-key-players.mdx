Private equity is not a solo endeavour. Every transaction involves a network of participants — fund managers, capital providers, advisers, lenders, and management teams — each with distinct roles, incentives, and influence over outcomes. Understanding who these players are and how they interact is essential context for making sense of PE deals and the industry's dynamics.

## The PE Firms (General Partners)

At the centre of the ecosystem are the PE firms themselves, acting as **general partners (GPs)**. These firms raise capital, source deals, execute acquisitions, manage portfolio companies, and deliver returns to investors.

The largest firms by **assets under management (AUM)** include:

- **Blackstone** — the world's largest alternative asset manager, with over $1 trillion in AUM across buyouts, real estate, credit, and infrastructure
- **KKR** — pioneer of the leveraged buyout, now managing over $500 billion across multiple strategies
- **Apollo Global Management** — known for its credit-oriented approach and willingness to take complex, contrarian positions
- **Carlyle Group** — strong in defence, aerospace, and government services; significant international footprint
- **TPG** — historically focused on consumer, healthcare, and technology sectors; went public in 2022

Beyond the mega-firms, there are hundreds of mid-market and specialist PE firms — sector-focused funds targeting healthcare, software, industrials, or financial services — and lower mid-market buyout shops that acquire businesses with $5–50 million of EBITDA.

## The Investors (Limited Partners)

**Limited partners (LPs)** provide the vast majority of capital that PE firms deploy. They are passive investors — they commit capital but have no role in investment decisions. The LP universe includes:

- **Pension funds** — the largest category of LP capital globally. Public pension funds (CalPERS, Ontario Teachers, USS in the UK) and corporate pension funds allocate 5–15% of their assets to private equity in pursuit of the long-term return premium over public markets.

- **Endowments** — university and institutional endowments (Yale, Harvard, Princeton) pioneered large PE allocations in the 1990s under David Swensen's influence. Yale's endowment model — heavy alternatives weighting — became the template for institutional investing.

- **Sovereign wealth funds (SWFs)** — state-owned investment vehicles managing national reserve capital. The Abu Dhabi Investment Authority (ADIA), GIC (Singapore), Temasek, and Norway's Government Pension Fund Global are among the largest PE LPs globally. **Sovereign wealth funds** bring enormous capital, long investment horizons, and geopolitical considerations to LP relationships.

- **Insurance companies** — allocate PE capital as part of their broader alternatives exposure, seeking illiquidity premium to match long-dated liabilities.

- **Family offices** — private investment vehicles for ultra-high-net-worth families. Single-family offices may commit $10–100 million to individual PE funds; multi-family offices aggregate commitments across multiple client families.

- **Fund of funds** — vehicles that invest in multiple PE funds rather than directly into companies. They provide diversification and access for smaller institutions but add a layer of fees.

## The Advisers

Every significant PE transaction involves a ring of professional advisers:

- **Investment banks** — advise on deal origination, financing, and exits. Investment banks run sell-side M&A processes, provide fairness opinions, and arrange debt financing for LBOs. The major bulge-bracket banks (Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan) and specialist M&A boutiques (Lazard, Evercore, Rothschild) are regular counterparties in PE deals.

- **Law firms** — handle deal documentation (purchase agreements, financing agreements, shareholder agreements), regulatory filings, and fund formation. Major PE law firms include Kirkland & Ellis, Latham & Watkins, and Simpson Thacher.

- **Management consultants** — often engaged during **due diligence** to assess market size, competitive dynamics, and operational improvement opportunities. McKinsey, Bain, and BCG have dedicated PE practices.

- **Accounting and transaction advisory firms** — conduct financial due diligence (quality of earnings analysis, working capital assessment) and tax structuring. The Big Four (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG) dominate this work.

## The Portfolio Company Management Teams

Once an acquisition closes, the existing management team (or a new team recruited by the PE firm) becomes a critical partner. **Management teams** typically receive equity incentives — a portion of the company's ownership — aligning their financial interests with the PE firm's return objectives.

The quality and motivation of management is consistently cited by PE investors as one of the most important determinants of deal success. PE firms that build deep relationships with talented operators — before they need them — have a structural advantage in deal sourcing and execution.

## The Lenders

**Leveraged buyouts** rely heavily on debt financing, provided by a range of lenders:

- **Banks** — provide senior secured loans (often syndicated across multiple lenders) at the lowest interest rates but with the most restrictive covenants
- **Direct lenders** — private credit funds (Ares, Owl Rock/Blue Owl, HPS) that provide unitranche or junior debt directly to borrowers, bypassing banks; faster and more flexible but more expensive
- **High-yield bond investors** — institutional buyers of below-investment-grade bonds issued by highly leveraged companies

## Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Assets under management (AUM)** — the total value of capital a firm manages on behalf of investors
- **Sovereign wealth fund** — a state-owned investment vehicle managing a country's reserve capital or surplus revenues
- **Endowment** — an institutional investment fund established to support a non-profit organisation (university, hospital) in perpetuity
- **Due diligence** — the comprehensive investigation of a target company before completing an acquisition, covering commercial, financial, legal, and operational dimensions
