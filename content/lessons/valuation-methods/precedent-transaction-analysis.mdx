**Precedent transaction analysis** (deal comps) values a business by examining the multiples paid in recent M&A transactions involving similar companies. Unlike trading comps (which reflect ongoing market valuations), deal comps reflect what actual buyers paid in completed acquisitions.

### How to Build a Precedent Transactions Analysis

**Step 1: Identify relevant transactions.** Search M&A databases (Capital IQ, PitchBook, Mergermarket, Bloomberg) for recent transactions in the same sector, of similar size, and ideally involving similar deal types (PE buyout, strategic acquisition, carve-out).

**Step 2: Collect deal data.** For each transaction, gather: enterprise value paid, target EBITDA at the time of the deal, target revenue, the acquirer type (strategic or financial), and the deal date.

**Step 3: Calculate transaction multiples.** The primary metric is EV/EBITDA at entry. Note whether EBITDA is reported (historical) or adjusted (the seller's figure used in the deal process), as adjusted EBITDA typically produces a lower multiple.

**Step 4: Analyse the range.** Examine the distribution of multiples across the precedent set. Consider the context of each deal: was it a competitive auction (likely pushed the price higher) or a proprietary deal (potentially lower)? Was the buyer strategic (willing to pay for synergies) or financial?

### Deal Comps vs. Trading Comps

Deal comps inherently include a **control premium** — the buyer paid a premium over the market price to acquire control. They also reflect the credit conditions at the time of each deal: transactions completed during cheap-credit periods (2006–2007, 2020–2021) tend to show higher multiples than those completed during tight-credit periods (2008–2009, 2022–2023).

In PE, deal comps are used to **defend entry pricing** ("this is in line with what buyers have paid for similar assets") and to calibrate exit expectations ("similar companies have been exiting at 12–14x EBITDA").

### Limitations

- Transaction data can be stale — a deal completed two years ago in a different interest rate environment may not be relevant
- Reported multiples may be based on adjusted EBITDA figures that are not publicly disclosed
- Small sample sizes in niche sectors make statistical analysis unreliable
- Synergy-driven strategic prices may overstate what a financial buyer should pay

### Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Precedent transactions** — valuation based on multiples paid in recent comparable M&A transactions
- **Deal comps** — shorthand for precedent transaction analysis
- **Control premium** — the premium inherent in acquisition prices over public trading valuations
- **Synergies** — cost savings or revenue enhancements a buyer expects to achieve post-acquisition; often justify higher acquisition multiples for strategic buyers
