**Comparable company analysis** (trading comps) values a business by comparing it to similar publicly traded companies. The logic is simple: if three comparable public companies trade at 12x EBITDA, a private company of similar quality should be worth approximately the same multiple — with adjustments for size, growth, and the absence of public market liquidity.

### How to Build a Trading Comps Analysis

**Step 1: Select comparable companies.** Identify 5–15 publicly listed companies that share key characteristics with the target: same industry, similar size, comparable growth profile, overlapping customer base, and similar business model. Perfect comparables are rare — judgement is required.

**Step 2: Gather financial data.** For each comparable, collect: enterprise value, revenue, EBITDA, EBIT, net income, and growth rates. Use consensus analyst estimates for forward-looking figures.

**Step 3: Calculate valuation multiples.** The primary multiples in PE are:
- **EV/EBITDA** — the most commonly used PE multiple; capital-structure neutral
- **EV/Revenue** — useful for high-growth or loss-making businesses where EBITDA is negative or distorted
- **P/E (Price/Earnings)** — less common in PE because it embeds capital structure effects

Calculate multiples on both trailing (last twelve months, LTM) and forward (next twelve months, NTM) basis. Forward multiples are generally more relevant because PE firms acquire businesses based on future earnings potential.

**Step 4: Determine the appropriate range.** Calculate mean, median, and interquartile range of the peer group multiples. The median is typically the most reliable central measure because it is less distorted by outliers.

**Step 5: Apply the multiple to the target.** Multiply the target's EBITDA by the selected multiple to derive an implied enterprise value. Apply the EV-to-equity bridge to derive equity value.

### Adjustments and Limitations

Trading comps reflect **minority, liquid valuations** — the price investors pay for small stakes in publicly traded companies with daily liquidity. This is different from PE, where buyers acquire controlling stakes in private companies with no liquidity. Two adjustments are necessary:

- **Control premium** — PE buyers typically pay 20–40% above the public trading price for control of a business, because control enables operational changes that minority shareholders cannot implement
- **Illiquidity discount** — some analysts argue private companies should trade at a discount to public peers because their shares cannot be easily sold; in practice, PE buyers rarely apply this discount explicitly because the control premium more than offsets it

Trading comps establish a **market floor** for valuation. They tell you what the market is willing to pay for similar businesses today.

### Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Trading comps** — valuation by comparison to publicly traded peer companies on a multiples basis
- **EV/EBITDA multiple** — enterprise value divided by EBITDA; the primary PE valuation multiple
- **Control premium** — the premium paid above the current market price to acquire a controlling stake in a business
- **LTM (Last Twelve Months)** — a trailing period used for historical multiple calculations
- **NTM (Next Twelve Months)** — a forward period used for forward multiple calculations
