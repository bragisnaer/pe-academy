A **discounted cash flow** analysis values a business based on the present value of its expected future cash flows. Unlike multiples-based approaches (which value by analogy), DCF is an **intrinsic valuation** — it asks: what is this specific business worth based on its own projected financial performance?

### The DCF Framework

A standard DCF has three components:

**1. Projection period (typically 5–10 years).** The analyst builds a detailed financial model projecting revenue, EBITDA, capex, working capital changes, and taxes to derive **unlevered free cash flow** for each year.

**2. Terminal value.** After the projection period, a terminal value captures the remaining value of the business in perpetuity. Two methods are common:

- **Perpetuity growth method:** Terminal value = Final year FCF x (1 + long-term growth rate) / (discount rate – long-term growth rate). Assumes cash flows grow at a stable rate indefinitely (typically 2–3%, approximating GDP growth).
- **Exit multiple method:** Terminal value = Final year EBITDA x assumed exit EV/EBITDA multiple. More common in PE because it mirrors how PE firms actually exit businesses.

**3. Discount rate.** Future cash flows are discounted to the present using the **weighted average cost of capital (WACC)** — a blended rate reflecting the cost of both debt and equity capital, weighted by the target capital structure.

> **Enterprise value = Sum of discounted FCFs + Discounted terminal value**

### Sensitivity Analysis

DCF outputs are highly sensitive to assumptions — small changes in growth rates, margins, or discount rates can shift the valuation by 20–30%. For this reason, PE professionals always run sensitivity analysis (also called a "sensitivity table" or "tornado chart") showing how the implied value changes across a range of assumptions.

The two most impactful assumptions are typically the **terminal growth rate** (or exit multiple) and the **discount rate**. A serious DCF analysis presents a valuation range, not a single point estimate.

### DCF in PE Practice

PE firms use DCF less frequently as a primary valuation tool than multiples analysis, because:

- PE deals are priced relative to market (multiples are what buyers actually pay)
- DCF requires many subjective assumptions that are hard to validate
- The terminal value often represents 60–80% of total DCF value, making the analysis heavily dependent on a single assumption about distant future performance

However, DCF is useful for:

- Cross-checking multiples-based valuations
- Valuing businesses in sectors where public comps are scarce
- Analysing asset-heavy businesses (infrastructure, real estate) where long-term cash flows are more predictable
- Defending or challenging a price in negotiations

### Exercise: DCF Sensitivity

**Task:** A target company has projected Year 1 FCF of €20 million, growing at 5% annually for 5 years. The terminal growth rate is 2.5% and the WACC is 10%. Using the perpetuity growth method, calculate the enterprise value.

**Solution:**
Year 1–5 FCF: €20.0M, €21.0M, €22.05M, €23.15M, €24.31M
Terminal value (end of Year 5): €24.31M x (1.025) / (0.10 – 0.025) = €24.92M / 0.075 = **€332.3M**
PV of Year 1–5 FCF (discounted at 10%): €18.18M + €17.36M + €16.57M + €15.82M + €15.10M = **€83.0M**
PV of terminal value: €332.3M / (1.10)^5 = €332.3M / 1.6105 = **€206.3M**
**Enterprise value = €83.0M + €206.3M = €289.3M**

Note that the terminal value represents 71% of total enterprise value — typical for a DCF, and the reason sensitivity analysis on terminal assumptions is critical.

### Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Discounted cash flow (DCF)** — valuation based on the present value of projected future cash flows
- **Terminal value** — the value of a business beyond the explicit projection period
- **WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital)** — the blended discount rate reflecting the cost of debt and equity capital
- **Perpetuity growth method** — calculating terminal value by assuming FCF grows at a constant rate indefinitely
- **Sensitivity analysis** — testing how valuation changes across a range of key assumptions
