Pricing is the most powerful profitability lever available. A 1% improvement in price, with volume held constant, typically flows straight to EBITDA. For a business with 20% EBITDA margins, a 1% price increase improves EBITDA by 5%. No other lever has the same ratio of effort to impact.

### Why Mid-Market Companies Underprice

Most mid-market companies acquired by PE have never conducted systematic pricing analysis. Common patterns:

**Cost-plus pricing.** The company calculates the cost of delivering a product or service, adds a standard margin (e.g., 30%), and charges the result. This ignores the value the customer receives and leaves money on the table when the customer would pay more.

**Legacy discounts.** Long-standing customers receive discounts negotiated years ago that no longer reflect the current value proposition or market conditions. These discounts compound: a 10% discount negotiated in 2015, never revisited, represents cumulative margin leakage across every subsequent year.

**Fear of customer loss.** Management avoids price increases because they fear losing customers. In reality, most B2B customers are far less price-sensitive than management assumes — particularly when the product or service is mission-critical and represents a small share of the customer's total cost base.

**No annual escalation mechanism.** The company has no systematic process for annual price reviews or inflation-linked increases.

### The PE Pricing Playbook

**Step 1: Price audit.** Map every customer by revenue, margin, and effective price. Identify the lowest-margin accounts and the widest price dispersion across similar customers.

**Step 2: Value-based repricing.** Where the company delivers differentiated value (speed, reliability, technical support, customisation), reprice to reflect that value rather than cost-plus.

**Step 3: Eliminate unjustified discounts.** Systematically review and reduce discounts that lack commercial justification.

**Step 4: Implement annual escalation.** Embed contractual price increase mechanisms (CPI-linked, fixed annual percentage) in all new and renewed contracts.

**Step 5: Differentiated pricing tiers.** Introduce service tiers (basic, premium, enterprise) that capture different willingness-to-pay segments.

### Pricing Case Example

A European testing and inspection services company acquired by a PE fund had not raised prices in 4 years. Post-acquisition pricing analysis revealed: (a) 25% of customers were below cost-to-serve; (b) no contractual price escalation clauses; (c) average price 12% below the two nearest competitors. The PE firm implemented a phased 8% price increase over 18 months with targeted account management to retain key customers. Customer attrition was under 3%. EBITDA improved by approximately €4 million annually on a €60 million revenue base — representing a 1.5x to 2x impact on enterprise value at exit.
