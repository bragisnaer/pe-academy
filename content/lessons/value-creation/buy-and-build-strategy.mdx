The **buy-and-build** strategy has become the dominant value creation playbook in private equity, particularly in the mid-market. Over 70% of PE-backed exits in recent years have involved platform companies that completed at least one bolt-on acquisition during the hold period.

### Why Buy-and-Build Works

**Multiple arbitrage** — Platform companies (typically acquired at 8–12x EBITDA) can acquire smaller businesses at 4–7x EBITDA. When the combined entity exits at the platform multiple, the bolt-ons are valued at a higher multiple than the PE firm paid, creating "free" value.

**Scale economies** — combining businesses creates procurement leverage, shared back-office functions, broader geographic coverage, and cross-selling opportunities.

**Market positioning** — consolidating a fragmented market creates a larger, more dominant player that commands higher valuations from strategic buyers at exit.

**Revenue synergies** — bolt-ons can bring complementary products, new customer segments, or geographic coverage that accelerates the platform's organic growth.

### Execution Risks

- **Integration failure** — acquiring companies is easier than integrating them. Cultural clashes, system incompatibilities, and customer disruption can destroy the value the acquisition was intended to create.
- **Overpayment** — as PE firms compete for bolt-on targets, prices have risen; multiple arbitrage is less reliable when bolt-ons cost 8–9x EBITDA.
- **Management bandwidth** — executing multiple acquisitions while running the base business strains management capacity.
- **Debt accumulation** — bolt-ons are often financed with incremental debt, increasing leverage during the hold period.

### Case Illustration: IFS (EQT)

EQT acquired IFS, a Swedish enterprise software company, in 2015. During ownership, EQT executed a deliberate buy-and-build and operational transformation, growing IFS through strategic acquisitions and a transition to a cloud-based subscription model. The company was sold to Advent International and subsequently to EQT again in a later fund, at significantly higher valuations reflecting the software-as-a-service transition and expanded market position.
