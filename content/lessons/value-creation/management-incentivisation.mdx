Aligning management's financial interests with the PE fund's return objectives is a foundational element of value creation. PE firms use equity incentive programmes to ensure management teams are motivated to maximise enterprise value.

### The Management Equity Programme (MEP)

Most PE-backed companies implement a **management equity programme (MEP)** (also called a management incentive plan, or MIP) in which key executives invest their own money to acquire equity alongside the PE fund.

Typical MEP structures:

- **Sweet equity / ratchet mechanisms** — management invests a relatively small amount but receives a disproportionate share of equity upside above a defined performance threshold (often 2x or 2.5x MOIC for the PE fund). This structure gives management significant upside participation if the business performs well while ensuring the PE fund achieves its minimum return first.

- **Co-investment at the same terms** — in some deals, management co-invests at the same price and terms as the PE fund. This provides direct alignment but less leveraged upside.

- **Options and phantom equity** — in jurisdictions where tax treatment favours it, management may receive stock options or phantom equity (cash-settled instruments tied to equity value) rather than actual shares.

### Vesting and Leaver Provisions

MEP equity typically vests over 3–5 years. If a manager departs before vesting is complete:

- **Good leaver** (termination without cause, retirement, death, disability) — the departing manager retains vested equity and may receive fair value for unvested shares.
- **Bad leaver** (voluntary resignation, termination for cause) — the departing manager forfeits all or most equity, often at cost or below fair value.

These provisions are critical because they retain key talent through the hold period and protect the PE fund from paying out equity to executives who leave before value creation is realised.
