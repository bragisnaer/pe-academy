Traditional deal sourcing relies on relationships, industry conferences, and investment bank intermediaries. Modern PE firms supplement these channels with data-driven sourcing — using technology to identify acquisition targets before they come to market.

### How Data-Driven Sourcing Works

**Data aggregation.** Firms collect structured and unstructured data from multiple sources: company registries (Companies House, Handelsregister, CVR in Denmark), financial databases (Capital IQ, PitchBook), job posting sites (indicating growth or leadership changes), news feeds, patent filings, web traffic data, customer review platforms, and social media signals.

**Pattern recognition.** Machine learning models identify patterns associated with attractive acquisition targets: companies in growing sectors that have reached a size threshold, businesses where founder age suggests succession planning, companies exhibiting growth acceleration or margin improvement, and businesses where ownership events (PE fund nearing end of life, corporate strategic review) suggest a potential sale.

**Scoring and prioritisation.** Each identified company is scored against the firm's investment criteria (sector, size, geography, growth profile, margin) and prioritised for outreach. The deal team receives a ranked pipeline of targets that meet their investment mandate, updated continuously.

### Case Illustration: EQT Motherbrain

EQT developed "Motherbrain," a proprietary AI-driven deal sourcing platform that ingests data from over 50 sources to identify potential investment targets. The system analyses millions of companies globally, tracking growth indicators, management changes, funding events, and other signals that suggest a company may be ready for PE investment. EQT has credited Motherbrain with sourcing multiple deals that the firm would not have identified through traditional channels.

Source: EQT press releases and technology updates, accessible at https://eqtgroup.com/digital

### Limitations

- Data-driven sourcing identifies candidates but cannot replace relationship-based sourcing. The initial contact, trust-building, and deal execution still require human judgement and interpersonal skills.
- Data quality varies across jurisdictions. Nordic and UK company registries provide rich, standardised data. Other markets (parts of Asia, Southern Europe, emerging markets) have less accessible or less reliable public data.
- AI models can produce false positives (companies that look attractive on data but are poor fits in practice) and miss non-obvious opportunities that a relationship-sourced deal would surface.
