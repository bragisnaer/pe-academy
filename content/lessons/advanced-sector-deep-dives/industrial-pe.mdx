Industrial PE covers manufacturing, industrial services, industrial distribution, logistics, and infrastructure services. The sector offers significant operational improvement potential but requires genuine manufacturing and supply chain expertise.

### Why PE Invests in Industrials

**Operational improvement potential.** Many mid-market industrial companies have not implemented lean manufacturing, supply chain optimisation, or modern ERP systems. PE operating partners with manufacturing backgrounds can drive significant efficiency gains.

**Fragmented markets.** Industrial distribution, specialised manufacturing, and industrial services are highly fragmented — creating buy-and-build consolidation opportunities.

**Tangible assets.** Industrial companies have real assets (machinery, inventory, real estate) that provide collateral for debt financing.

**Cyclicality management.** While cyclicality is a risk, experienced industrial PE investors use it strategically: acquiring companies at trough valuations, driving operational improvements through the hold period, and exiting at mid-cycle or peak.

### Industrial-Specific Metrics

- **Order intake / book-to-bill ratio:** Orders received vs. revenue delivered. A book-to-bill above 1.0x signals growing demand.
- **Backlog:** The value of contracted but undelivered orders. A growing backlog provides revenue visibility.
- **Capacity utilisation:** Actual production vs. maximum capacity. Below 70% signals excess capacity (cost reduction opportunity); above 90% signals expansion needed (capex requirement).
- **OEE (Overall Equipment Effectiveness):** A composite metric of availability x performance x quality. World-class manufacturing achieves 85%+ OEE; many mid-market manufacturers operate at 50–65%.
- **Working capital intensity:** Working capital as a percentage of revenue. Industrial businesses typically have higher working capital intensity (15–25% of revenue) than services or software businesses (5–10%).

### The Industrial PE Playbook

**Lean operations.** Implementing lean manufacturing principles (waste reduction, setup time reduction, flow optimisation, quality improvement). Can improve margins by 2–5 percentage points over 2–3 years.

**Procurement consolidation.** Centralising purchasing across a buy-and-build platform. A 3–5% reduction in direct material costs flows directly to EBITDA.

**Commercial excellence.** Many industrial companies are excellent at manufacturing but poor at selling. Implementing CRM, professionalising the sales function, and building key account management for top customers can accelerate revenue growth.

**Aftermarket and services.** Shifting revenue mix from equipment sales (one-time, cyclical) to aftermarket services (recurring, higher margin). Spare parts, maintenance contracts, and technical services provide more predictable revenue streams.
