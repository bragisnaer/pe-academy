Software has become the single largest PE sector by deal volume, driven by recurring revenue models, high margins, scalable operations, and the structural shift from on-premise to cloud delivery. Firms like Thoma Bravo, Vista Equity Partners, Hg Capital, and Insight Partners have built dedicated platforms around software buyouts.

### Why PE Loves Software

**Recurring revenue.** Subscription (SaaS) and maintenance revenue streams are predictable, contractual, and high-margin. A software company with 90% recurring revenue and 95% gross retention is essentially a cash flow machine — ideal for supporting leverage.

**High margins.** Mature software companies generate 25–50% EBITDA margins. Unlike manufacturing, software has minimal marginal cost — the 1,000th customer uses the same code as the first.

**Scalability.** Revenue can grow without proportional cost increase. A sales team can sell to more customers without rebuilding the product. Cloud delivery eliminates the need for on-premise installation and support.

**Switching costs.** Enterprise software embedded in customer workflows (ERP, CRM, HR, vertical-specific tools) is expensive and disruptive to replace. This creates high customer retention and pricing power.

### Software-Specific Metrics

PE professionals evaluating software companies use metrics that do not apply in other sectors:

- **ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue):** The annualised value of active recurring contracts. The primary revenue metric for SaaS businesses.
- **Net Revenue Retention (NRR):** Revenue from existing customers this year / revenue from the same customers last year. NRR above 110% means the company is growing revenue from existing customers (through upselling and price increases) faster than it is losing revenue from churn. Top software companies achieve 120–130% NRR.
- **Gross Revenue Retention (GRR):** Same calculation but excluding expansion revenue — measures only the churn effect. GRR above 90% is strong.
- **CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost):** Total sales and marketing cost to acquire a new customer.
- **LTV (Lifetime Value):** Total revenue expected from a customer over the entire relationship. LTV/CAC ratio above 3x is the standard benchmark for healthy unit economics.
- **CAC Payback Period:** Months required to recover the acquisition cost of a new customer from their subscription payments. Below 18 months is efficient.
- **Rule of 40:** Revenue growth rate + EBITDA margin should exceed 40%. A company growing 30% with 15% margins (45%) satisfies the rule. A company growing 5% with 20% margins (25%) does not. This metric balances growth and profitability.

### Software PE Valuation

Software companies are valued on **EV/ARR** (for high-growth, lower-margin businesses) or **EV/EBITDA** (for mature, profitable businesses). High-growth SaaS companies (30%+ ARR growth) can trade at 10–20x ARR. Mature software businesses with stable growth (5–15%) trade at 15–25x EBITDA.

### The Software PE Playbook

**Perpetual-to-subscription transition.** Many legacy software companies still sell perpetual licences (one-time payment plus annual maintenance). Transitioning to subscription pricing increases the predictability of revenue, improves NRR, and commands higher valuation multiples. The transition creates a short-term revenue dip (subscriptions are recognised over time rather than upfront) but a long-term value increase.

**Operational efficiency (the "Vista playbook").** Vista Equity Partners pioneered a standardised operational improvement programme for software companies: professionalising sales processes, implementing best-practice R&D methodologies, optimising customer support, and centralising back-office functions. The goal is to expand EBITDA margins from 20–25% to 35–45% through operating discipline.

**Consolidation.** Fragmented software verticals (dental practice management, property management, field service management) offer buy-and-build opportunities. Acquiring 5–10 smaller software companies and integrating them onto a single platform creates a market leader with scale, cross-selling potential, and premium exit multiples.
