Financial services PE is a specialist strategy that requires deep understanding of regulatory capital, capital adequacy, and the unique dynamics of financial institutions.

### Financial Services Sub-Sectors for PE

**Insurance:** Life, property & casualty, specialty, and reinsurance. PE firms (Apollo, KKR, Carlyle) have been active acquirers of insurance companies, primarily for access to the "float" — policyholder premiums that can be invested in the PE firm's own credit and alternative strategies.

**Payments and fintech:** Payment processors, merchant acquirers, payment infrastructure. Highly attractive to PE due to recurring transaction-based revenue, high margins, and structural growth from the shift from cash to digital payments.

**Wealth and asset management:** Independent financial advisers, wealth management platforms, and asset management firms. Buy-and-build consolidation of fragmented adviser networks is a well-established PE strategy.

**Specialty finance:** Consumer lending, equipment leasing, factoring, trade finance. Asset-backed businesses with predictable loss rates and recurring revenue.

### Regulatory Considerations

> **Market-specific note:** Financial services regulation is highly jurisdiction-specific.

Financial services companies are regulated by national authorities (FCA in the UK, BaFin in Germany, Finanstilsynet in Denmark, SEC/OCC/Fed in the US). PE acquisitions of regulated financial institutions require regulatory approval, and ongoing capital adequacy, conduct, and reporting requirements apply. Change-of-control approvals can take 3–12 months and may be conditional.

### Financial Services Valuation

Financial services companies are often valued on **price/book value** or **price/earnings** rather than EV/EBITDA, because their balance sheets (assets and liabilities) are the core of the business model.

Insurance companies are valued on embedded value (the present value of expected future cash flows from in-force policies) and P/E multiples.

Payment companies are valued on EV/Revenue or EV/EBITDA, similar to software, reflecting their recurring, transaction-based revenue.

### Key Terms Introduced in This Module

- **ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue)** — the annualised value of active recurring contracts; the primary SaaS revenue metric
- **NRR (Net Revenue Retention)** — year-over-year revenue from existing customers, including expansion and churn; above 110% is strong
- **Rule of 40** — revenue growth rate + EBITDA margin; above 40% indicates a healthy software business
- **Reimbursement risk** — the risk that government or insurance payment rates change, affecting healthcare provider revenues
- **OEE (Overall Equipment Effectiveness)** — availability x performance x quality; a composite manufacturing efficiency metric
- **Book-to-bill ratio** — orders received divided by revenue delivered; above 1.0x signals growing demand
- **Float** — policyholder premiums held by an insurance company before claims are paid; available for investment
