Understanding how accounting choices affect EBITDA is the connective tissue between accounting knowledge and PE deal analysis.

### The Adjusted EBITDA Checklist

For every target company, PE professionals should verify:

1. **Revenue recognition:** Is revenue being recognised at the appropriate time? Are there channel-stuffing or bill-and-hold concerns?
2. **Lease treatment:** Is the EBITDA figure pre- or post-IFRS 16? Are lease liabilities included in the EV bridge?
3. **SBC:** Is SBC added back? What is the actual equity dilution from SBC programmes?
4. **Capitalisation vs. expensing:** Is the company capitalising costs that should be expensed (e.g., capitalising R&D or maintenance costs to reduce operating expenses and inflate EBITDA)?
5. **Provision movements:** Are provisions being created and released to smooth earnings? A company that builds a large provision in one year and releases it the next can make a weak year look like a normal year.
6. **Related-party transactions:** Are intercompany transactions priced at market rates? Related-party purchases above market inflate costs; related-party sales below market deflate revenue.
7. **Foreign currency:** How are FX gains and losses treated? Are they included in or excluded from adjusted EBITDA?
8. **Pension accounting:** Are pension costs included in EBITDA? Under IFRS, the net interest cost on defined benefit pension obligations can be classified as a finance cost (below EBITDA) rather than an operating cost.

### Exercise: Accounting Adjustment Identification

**Task:** A target company reports the following:
- Revenue: €80M (up 15% year-over-year)
- Accounts receivable: €22M (up 35% year-over-year)
- EBITDA: €18M
- SBC expense: €2M (added back to arrive at adjusted EBITDA of €20M)
- The company switched from expensing software development costs to capitalising them mid-year (€1.5M capitalised)
- IFRS 16 lease liabilities: €12M (not included in the EV bridge in the seller's materials)

Identify every accounting concern and calculate your adjusted EBITDA.

**Solution guidance:**
1. AR growing at 35% while revenue grows at 15% suggests possible revenue recognition acceleration. Investigate.
2. SBC add-back of €2M is debatable. Accept for EBITDA but account for dilution separately.
3. Software development capitalisation of €1.5M inflates EBITDA (cost moved from opex to capex). Reverse the capitalisation: deduct €1.5M from adjusted EBITDA.
4. IFRS 16 lease liabilities (€12M) must be included as debt-like items in the EV bridge.

Your adjusted EBITDA: €18M – €1.5M capitalisation reversal = **€16.5M** (or €18.5M if you accept the SBC add-back). Compared to the seller's €20M, this represents a 10–17.5% reduction.

### Key Terms Introduced in This Module

- **IFRS 15 / ASC 606** — the converged revenue recognition standard; five-step framework for determining when revenue is recognised
- **IFRS 16 / ASC 842** — lease accounting standards bringing most leases onto the balance sheet
- **Right-of-use asset (ROU)** — the balance sheet asset representing the lessee's right to use a leased asset
- **Purchase price allocation (PPA)** — the allocation of an acquisition's purchase price to identified assets, liabilities, and goodwill
- **Goodwill** — the excess of the purchase price over the fair value of identified net assets
- **Stock-based compensation (SBC)** — compensation paid in equity (shares or options) rather than cash; a non-cash charge with real economic cost through dilution
- **Capitalisation vs. expensing** — the choice to record a cost as a balance sheet asset (capitalised) rather than an income statement expense; affects EBITDA timing
