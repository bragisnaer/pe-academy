Stock-based compensation (SBC) is a recurring cost for many PE portfolio companies, particularly technology and software businesses. Its accounting treatment directly affects adjusted EBITDA and is a frequent area of disagreement between buyers and sellers.

### The Debate: Is SBC a "Real" Cost?

**The argument for adding back SBC:** SBC is a non-cash charge. It does not consume cash in the period it is recognised. Many PE firms and sell-side analysts add back SBC to arrive at adjusted EBITDA.

**The argument against adding back SBC:** SBC dilutes equity holders. If the company issues shares to employees as compensation, existing shareholders own a smaller percentage of the business. The economic cost is real — it is just paid in equity rather than cash. Berkshire Hathaway's Warren Buffett famously stated that treating SBC as a non-expense is intellectually dishonest.

### PE Practice

Most PE firms add back SBC for EBITDA calculation purposes but account for the dilutive effect separately:
- Adjusted EBITDA excludes SBC (treating it as non-cash)
- The LBO model accounts for management equity separately (the MEP/MIP equity pool, typically 10–20% of the company, is treated as a dilution to the sponsor's equity return)
- When comparing multiples, the buyer should be aware of whether the target's peers include or exclude SBC in their reported EBITDA

### Post-Acquisition SBC

Under PE ownership, SBC typically takes the form of the management equity programme (MEP) rather than publicly traded stock options. The accounting treatment of MEP equity varies:
- If management acquires shares at fair market value, no SBC charge is recognised (they paid for the shares)
- If management receives shares below fair value or with ratchet features, the discount is recognised as a compensation expense under IFRS 2 / ASC 718
- Sweet equity with performance conditions requires complex fair value measurement and expense recognition over the vesting period
