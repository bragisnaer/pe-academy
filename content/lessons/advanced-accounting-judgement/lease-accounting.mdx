Lease accounting has a direct impact on PE valuation because it affects how much of a company's cost base is visible on the balance sheet and how EBITDA is calculated.

### IFRS 16 (Effective 2019)

Under IFRS 16, virtually all leases are recognised on the balance sheet:
- A **right-of-use asset** (ROU asset) is recognised on the asset side
- A **lease liability** is recognised on the liability side (the present value of future lease payments)
- In the income statement, the operating lease expense (rent) is replaced by **depreciation** of the ROU asset and **interest** on the lease liability

### Impact on EBITDA

Under IFRS 16, EBITDA increases because the lease expense (previously an operating cost above EBITDA) is split into depreciation (below EBITDA) and interest (below EBITDA). This can be significant for companies with large lease portfolios (retailers, restaurant chains, logistics companies).

**Example:** A retail chain with €20M annual rent. Pre-IFRS 16 EBITDA: €30M. Post-IFRS 16 EBITDA: approximately €50M (because €20M of rent is now below the EBITDA line). The enterprise value has not changed, but the EBITDA multiple has compressed from potentially misleading levels.

### Impact on Enterprise Value

Under IFRS 16, lease liabilities are on the balance sheet and should be treated as debt-like items in the EV bridge. Enterprise value = Equity value + Net debt + Lease liabilities. If lease liabilities are excluded from the EV bridge, the buyer is effectively paying for the leases twice (once in the EV multiple applied to post-IFRS 16 EBITDA, and once in the ongoing lease payments).

### PE Practice

Most PE professionals adjust for IFRS 16 by using either:
- **Pre-IFRS 16 EBITDA** (adding lease payments back above the EBITDA line, consistent with pre-2019 treatment)
- **Post-IFRS 16 EBITDA** but including lease liabilities as debt-like items in the EV bridge

Consistency is critical. Comparing a target's post-IFRS 16 EBITDA to pre-IFRS 16 trading comps produces misleading multiples.

> **US GAAP note (ASC 842):** US GAAP distinguishes between **finance leases** (on-balance-sheet, similar to IFRS 16) and **operating leases** (recognised on the balance sheet as ROU assets and liabilities, but the income statement treatment remains as a single lease expense in operating costs — unlike IFRS 16's depreciation + interest split). This means EBITDA under US GAAP is less affected by lease accounting than under IFRS.
