GP-led secondaries have evolved from a niche transaction type to a $50+ billion annual market. Understanding the mechanics, conflicts, and economics is increasingly important.

### Detailed Mechanics

1. The GP identifies one or more portfolio companies in an existing fund (Fund IV, approaching end of life) that still have significant value creation potential
2. The GP creates a new continuation vehicle (SPV) to acquire these assets
3. The GP engages a financial adviser (typically a secondary advisory firm like Evercore, Lazard, PJT Partners, or Jefferies) to run the transaction
4. Existing Fund IV LPs receive an offer: cash out at a specified price (typically NAV or a small premium/discount) or roll their investment into the new vehicle
5. New investors (secondary funds) commit capital to the continuation vehicle, funding the cash-out for exiting LPs and potentially providing additional capital for the portfolio company's growth plan
6. The GP retains management and earns new management fees and carried interest on the continuation vehicle

### Conflicts of Interest

GP-led secondaries create inherent conflicts because the GP is on both sides of the transaction:
- The GP is the seller (from the existing fund) and the manager (of the new vehicle)
- The GP has an incentive to set the transfer price low (to benefit the new vehicle's returns and the GP's new carry pool) but also an obligation to existing LPs to achieve fair value
- The GP earns new management fees and carry on the continuation vehicle, creating an economic incentive to pursue these transactions regardless of LP interest

### Safeguards

To manage these conflicts, best practice requires:
- **Independent LPAC approval** — the LP advisory committee reviews and approves the transaction
- **Third-party fairness opinion** — an independent valuation confirms the transfer price is fair
- **Full transparency** — existing LPs receive comprehensive information about the asset, the GP's plan, and the terms of the new vehicle
- **Genuine optionality** — LPs must have a real choice to cash out or roll; the cash-out option must be funded
- **Independent LP representation** — some transactions appoint an independent LP representative to negotiate terms on behalf of existing LPs
