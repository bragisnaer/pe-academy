Institutional LPs conduct rigorous due diligence before committing to a PE fund. Understanding what LPs look for helps GP professionals present their firm effectively and helps LP-side professionals evaluate fund opportunities.

### The LP Evaluation Framework

**Quantitative assessment:**
- **Net IRR and net MOIC** across all prior fund vintages. LPs look for consistency, not just one strong vintage. A firm that delivered 25% net IRR in Fund II but 8% in Fund III raises questions about repeatability.
- **DPI** — how much cash has actually been returned? High TVPI with low DPI (heavy unrealised value) may indicate either early-stage funds or optimistic valuations.
- **Dispersion** — are returns concentrated in a few outlier deals, or broadly distributed across the portfolio? Heavy reliance on a single home run suggests luck rather than repeatable skill.
- **PME (Public Market Equivalent)** — did the fund outperform the public markets? A fund with a 15% net IRR sounds good, but if the S&P 500 returned 14% over the same period with zero fees and daily liquidity, the PE premium is negligible.
- **Loss ratio** — what percentage of deals lost money? A top-quartile fund might have a loss ratio below 15%; a bottom-quartile fund above 35%.

**Qualitative assessment:**
- **Team stability** — have the key investment professionals (partners who sourced, led, and exited the strong deals) remained? If the partners who generated the track record have left and been replaced, the historical returns are not attributable to the current team.
- **Attribution** — which individuals led which deals? Can specific partners credibly claim credit for the returns?
- **Strategy evolution** — has the strategy changed across fund generations? A firm that was a generalist mid-market fund in Fund I but is now raising a technology-focused growth equity fund in Fund IV needs to demonstrate credibility in the new strategy.
- **Culture and governance** — is the firm well-managed as a business? How are disputes resolved? How is carry allocated internally? Internal disputes over carry allocation have destroyed several PE firms.
- **Succession planning** — does the firm have a credible next-generation team? Firms dependent on a single founder face existential key-person risk.

### The LP Decision Process

Most institutional LPs follow a multi-stage evaluation:

1. **Screening** — does the fund fit the LP's allocation strategy (sector, geography, fund size, return target)?
2. **Preliminary review** — review of PPM, track record, and initial meeting with the GP
3. **Deep diligence** — extensive meetings (typically 4–8 hours across multiple sessions), reference calls (15–20 references including portfolio company executives, co-investors, lenders, and LPs from prior funds), site visits, and quantitative analysis
4. **Investment committee approval** — the LP's internal committee reviews the diligence findings and votes on the commitment
5. **Side letter negotiation** — large LPs negotiate individual terms (fee discounts, co-investment access, reporting enhancements, MFN clauses) documented in a side letter

The entire process typically takes 3–6 months from first meeting to commitment.
