### The Pattern

Several major PE firms have experienced destabilising internal disputes over carried interest allocation — how the carry pool is divided among partners and senior professionals. These disputes rarely become public, but when they do, they reveal the tensions inherent in PE's partnership structure.

### Common Conflict Patterns

**Founder vs. next generation.** As PE firms institutionalise, founding partners who built the firm expect to retain a dominant share of carry. Rising partners who are now sourcing and executing deals expect their share to grow. If the founder holds 40–50% of the carry pool and resists redistribution, top-performing next-generation partners may leave — often taking LP relationships and deal flow with them.

**Deal attribution disputes.** Who "owns" a deal? The partner who sourced it? The VP who ran the diligence and managed the portfolio company for 5 years? The operating partner who implemented the cost savings that drove the return? Carry allocation debates often turn on attribution — and attribution is inherently subjective.

**Performance vs. seniority.** Should carry reflect current contribution or accumulated seniority? A young partner who sourced and led the fund's best-performing deal may receive less carry than a senior partner who contributed less to the current fund but holds a legacy allocation from prior funds.

### Case Illustration

The most publicised example is the departure of David Bonderman and Jim Coulter's different approaches at TPG, though the firm has navigated transitions. More broadly, Carlyle Group experienced significant senior departures in the mid-2010s partly attributed to internal compensation dynamics, and numerous mid-market firms have split over carry allocation disputes that never made public headlines.

### Lessons

- Carry allocation frameworks should be documented, transparent (at least internally), and reviewed regularly
- Succession planning is inseparable from carry allocation — the next generation will not stay without a credible path to meaningful carry
- Firms that handle internal economics poorly lose their best people, which degrades returns, which makes the next fundraise harder — a destructive cycle
- LP due diligence should assess team stability and carry allocation as a governance indicator
