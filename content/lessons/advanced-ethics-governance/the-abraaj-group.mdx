### What Happened

The Abraaj Group, founded by Arif Naqvi in 2002, was the largest PE firm in the Middle East and one of the most prominent emerging market investors globally. At its peak, Abraaj managed approximately $14 billion across funds focused on Africa, South Asia, and the Middle East. The firm cultivated a reputation for impact investing — its Abraaj Growth Markets Health Fund, backed by the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, was designed to invest in healthcare infrastructure across developing countries.

In 2018, an investigation by the Wall Street Journal revealed that Abraaj had commingled LP fund capital with the firm's corporate accounts and had used LP money earmarked for healthcare investments to cover the firm's own operating expenses and service its corporate debt. The Gates Foundation and other LPs in the healthcare fund raised concerns when capital calls were issued for investments that appeared not to have been made.

Abraaj collapsed in 2018 and entered liquidation. Arif Naqvi was arrested in 2019, extradited to the US, and in 2023 was convicted of fraud, racketeering, and money laundering. He was sentenced to prison.

### Governance Failures

- **Commingling of funds.** The most fundamental rule in fund management is that LP capital is held separately from the GP's corporate funds. Abraaj violated this rule systematically.
- **Inadequate LP oversight.** Despite being one of the largest emerging market PE firms, Abraaj's LP base did not detect the misuse of funds for years. LP reporting was accepted at face value without independent verification.
- **Auditor failure.** Abraaj's auditors (KPMG initially, later replaced) did not flag the commingling or the discrepancies between reported and actual fund investments.
- **Depositary absence.** In AIFMD-regulated jurisdictions, a requirement for an independent depositary to hold fund assets would have prevented or detected the commingling. Abraaj's funds were domiciled in the Cayman Islands, where depositary requirements were less stringent.

### Lessons

- LP due diligence must go beyond track record analysis to include verification of fund governance, custody arrangements, and operational controls
- Independent depositaries (required under AIFMD in Europe) serve as a critical safeguard against fund misuse
- Impact investing credentials do not exempt a firm from basic governance scrutiny
- Regulatory gaps in offshore fund domiciles can enable misconduct
