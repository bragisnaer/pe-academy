### What Happened

Cerberus Capital Management acquired Steward Health Care System, a network of community hospitals in the northeastern United States, in 2010. Under PE ownership, Steward pursued aggressive growth (expanding from 6 to 35+ hospitals) and cost optimisation. In 2016–2017, Cerberus extracted approximately $800 million through sale-leaseback transactions on Steward's hospital real estate — the properties were sold to Medical Properties Trust (a REIT) and leased back to Steward.

The sale-leaseback generated immediate cash for Cerberus but transformed Steward from a hospital operator that owned its buildings into a hospital operator paying rent. Annual lease obligations consumed cash that might otherwise have been invested in equipment, staffing, and patient care.

By 2023–2024, Steward was in severe financial distress. Hospitals reported critical supply shortages, unpaid vendor invoices, staffing crises, and deteriorating patient care conditions. In May 2024, Steward filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy. Multiple state attorneys general and the US Senate investigated whether PE-driven financial extraction had compromised patient safety.

### Ethical Tensions

- **Sale-leaseback extraction.** The $800 million extracted through real estate transactions weakened Steward's financial position. While sale-leasebacks are a legitimate financing tool, the scale of extraction relative to the business's capacity to absorb the resulting lease obligations raises ethical questions.
- **Cost cutting in healthcare.** Staffing reductions and deferred equipment investment in hospitals can directly affect patient outcomes. The profit motive in PE ownership intersects with a social obligation to provide adequate care.
- **Regulatory accountability.** Healthcare is a regulated industry with public-interest obligations. PE ownership of healthcare providers creates a tension between return targets and care standards that does not exist in most other sectors.

### Lessons

- PE ownership of essential services (healthcare, social care, education) requires heightened governance and ethical scrutiny
- Sale-leaseback transactions must be assessed not only for financial impact but for whether they leave the operating business sustainable
- Regulatory and political scrutiny of PE in healthcare is increasing globally and will likely result in sector-specific regulation

Source: US Senate Committee on Health, Education, Labor and Pensions investigation and hearings (2024); reporting by the Boston Globe, New York Times, and Wall Street Journal.
