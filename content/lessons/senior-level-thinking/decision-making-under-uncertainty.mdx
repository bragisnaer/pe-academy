The most important skill in PE is not financial modelling — it is judgement. Every investment decision is made with incomplete information, under time pressure, with real capital at risk. Senior PE professionals distinguish themselves not by avoiding uncertainty, but by structuring decisions that perform well across a range of outcomes.

### The Pre-Mortem

Before committing to a deal, the best investment committees conduct a **pre-mortem**: asking the question "assume this deal fails — what went wrong?" This reversal forces the team to identify the most likely failure modes rather than seeking confirmation of their thesis.

Common failure modes to pre-mortem:
- The market growth assumed in the model does not materialise
- A key customer is lost in the first 18 months
- The management team cannot execute the value creation plan
- Interest rates rise significantly, increasing debt service costs
- A bolt-on acquisition fails to integrate
- A competitor enters the market or disrupts the business model

For each failure mode, the team should assess: (a) the probability, (b) the impact on returns if it occurs, and (c) whether there is a mitigation or contingency plan.

### Scenario Planning in Practice

PE firms use scenario analysis to test the robustness of their thesis. A standard approach includes:

**Base case** — management's operating plan, adjusted for the deal team's independent assessment of risk. This is the case the investment committee approves.

**Upside case** — what happens if everything goes right? Revenue growth exceeds plan, margin improvement is faster than expected, the exit market is strong. This case should be plausible, not aspirational.

**Downside case** — what happens if the market turns, costs rise, or execution stumbles? The downside case should not be a moderate softening of the base case. It should model a genuine adverse scenario — a recession, a customer loss, a technology disruption.

**Stress case** — what happens in a truly adverse environment? Can the company service its debt if EBITDA falls 20%? What if the exit multiple compresses by 2 turns? At what point does the equity get wiped out?

The investment committee should spend more time on the downside and stress cases than on the base case. The base case tells you why the deal works. The downside and stress cases tell you how much you can lose — and whether the risk-adjusted return justifies the commitment.

### Kill Discipline

The discipline to walk away from deals — particularly after investing significant time and money in diligence — is a defining trait of top-performing PE firms. Studies consistently show that the best-performing funds have higher "kill rates" (percentage of deals screened that are not pursued) than mediocre funds.

**Why walking away is hard:**
- Sunk cost bias — the team has spent months and hundreds of thousands in diligence fees
- Competitive pressure — if you do not bid, someone else will
- Revenue pressure — the firm's fee income depends on deploying capital
- Ego — the deal team has emotionally committed to the transaction

**Why walking away is essential:**
- The entry price determines the exit return. Overpaying at entry is the single most common cause of underperformance.
- Every dollar deployed in a mediocre deal is a dollar not available for a strong one.
- Discipline compounds — firms known for kill discipline attract better deal flow because sellers know an offer from that firm is serious.
