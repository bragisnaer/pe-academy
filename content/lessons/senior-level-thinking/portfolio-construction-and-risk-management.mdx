Individual deal quality matters, but fund-level returns are determined by **portfolio construction** — how deals are assembled across sectors, geographies, vintages, and risk profiles.

### Diversification Principles

A well-constructed PE fund portfolio balances:

- **Sector diversity** — exposure across 3–5 sectors reduces the risk that a downturn in one sector destroys fund returns
- **Geographic diversity** — for pan-European or global funds, spreading investments across countries mitigates country-specific risks
- **Vintage diversity** — investing consistently across the fund's deployment period (rather than concentrating in a single year) reduces timing risk
- **Deal size distribution** — a mix of larger platform investments and smaller bolt-on opportunities; concentration limits (typically 15–25% of fund capital per deal) prevent single-deal dependency

### Portfolio-Level Risk Management

**Leverage monitoring.** Tracking leverage ratios, interest coverage, and covenant headroom across all portfolio companies on a quarterly basis. Companies approaching covenant breach require immediate GP intervention.

**Sector correlation.** If multiple portfolio companies are exposed to the same cyclical risk (e.g., consumer discretionary spending), the portfolio is less diversified than it appears.

**Currency exposure.** For multi-currency funds, aggregate FX exposure across the portfolio should be monitored and managed.

**Exit pipeline management.** Maintaining a rolling exit calendar to avoid the concentration of multiple exits in the same quarter or year (which creates execution risk and LP distribution lumpiness).

### Downside Protection

The best PE firms are defined not by their best deals but by their worst. A single write-off can destroy a fund's net returns. Downside protection strategies include:

- Conservative entry leverage (even when credit markets allow more)
- Diverse revenue base in portfolio companies (no single-customer dependency)
- Committed financing before signing (avoiding "hung" deals that fail to fund)
- Holding adequate reserves for portfolio company needs (follow-on capital, working capital support)
- Active board governance to detect problems early
