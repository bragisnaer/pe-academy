### Entry Points

**Investment banking** remains the most common entry path into PE at the associate level. Analysts at bulge-bracket or elite boutique banks are the primary recruiting pool for PE associate positions.

**Management consulting** (McKinsey, Bain, BCG) is the second most common path, particularly for firms that value operational capabilities alongside financial skills.

**Industry operators** — former CEOs, CFOs, and functional leaders — are increasingly recruited as operating partners or portfolio company executives.

**Direct-from-undergraduate** programmes exist at some larger firms but are less common.

**MBA** remains a viable entry point, particularly for career changers from non-finance backgrounds.

### Career Progression

The path from analyst to partner typically takes 12–15 years. The critical inflection points are:

- **Analyst to Associate:** Demonstrating mastery of financial modelling, diligence coordination, and working autonomously on deal workstreams.
- **Associate to VP:** Showing ability to manage a deal process end-to-end, not just execute individual workstreams. Beginning to develop client and industry relationships.
- **VP to Principal/Director:** Demonstrating deal origination capability — the ability to source proprietary deal flow through personal relationships. This is the highest-attrition level.
- **Principal to Partner:** Earning the trust of existing partners to share capital allocation authority, LP relationships, and firm leadership responsibility. Requires demonstrated deal track record, leadership capability, and alignment with firm culture.

### Skills That Differentiate

Beyond technical finance skills (which are table stakes), the professionals who reach the top of PE consistently demonstrate:

- **Judgement** — the ability to synthesise incomplete information into a clear investment recommendation, with explicit articulation of what could go wrong
- **Relationship-building** — deal sourcing is a relationship business; the best investors build networks across industry operators, intermediaries, and co-investors that produce proprietary deal flow
- **Operational instinct** — understanding how businesses actually work, not just how they model; time spent with portfolio company management teams builds this
- **Communication** — the ability to present complex information clearly and persuasively to investment committees, management teams, and LPs
- **Resilience** — the ability to maintain conviction during periods of adversity (credit crunches, portfolio company crises, deal failures) while remaining intellectually honest about when to cut losses

### Key Terms Introduced in This Module

- **Investment thesis** — the structured articulation of why a specific investment will generate the target return
- **Portfolio construction** — the composition of investments across a fund, managed for diversification and risk control
- **Semi-liquid PE products** — PE fund structures offering periodic liquidity to investors, expanding access beyond institutions
- **Fiduciary duty** — the legal obligation of the GP to act in the best interests of LP investors
