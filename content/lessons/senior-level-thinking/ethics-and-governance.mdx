PE firms manage other people's money. The ethical obligations that flow from this responsibility are significant and extend beyond legal compliance.

### LP Fiduciary Duty

GPs owe a fiduciary duty to LPs — an obligation to act in LPs' best interests, avoid self-dealing, and manage conflicts of interest transparently. Common areas of ethical tension:

- **Fee allocation** — which expenses are charged to the fund versus the GP? Historically, some GPs charged questionable expenses (monitoring fees, internal advisory fees) to portfolio companies. LP pressure and SEC enforcement have curtailed these practices.
- **Conflicts between funds** — if a GP manages multiple funds, which fund gets access to the best deal opportunities? How are co-investment allocations distributed fairly?
- **Valuation integrity** — GPs have an economic incentive to value portfolio companies aggressively (higher NAV improves reported performance and supports the next fundraise). Honest valuation — including writing down underperforming investments promptly — is an ethical obligation.
- **Carried interest timing** — structuring exits to accelerate carry crystallisation (benefiting GP economics) rather than maximising LP returns.

### The Social Licence to Operate

PE's reputation affects its ability to attract capital, recruit talent, and win deals. The industry's social licence depends on demonstrating that PE ownership creates value for all stakeholders — not just investors.

Issues that threaten the social licence:

- **Job losses.** PE-backed companies that aggressively cut headcount draw public and political criticism. While some workforce optimisation is legitimate, patterns of short-term cost-cutting that undermine long-term business health damage the industry's reputation.
- **Tax efficiency.** PE structures that minimise tax — particularly the favourable tax treatment of carried interest (taxed as capital gains rather than income in many jurisdictions) — generate political controversy. The carried interest tax debate has been ongoing for over two decades.
- **Debt loading.** Companies that collapse under PE-imposed debt burden (e.g., Toys "R" Us in the US, Debenhams in the UK) generate headlines and regulatory attention.
- **Healthcare and essential services.** PE ownership of hospitals, care homes, and emergency services attracts particular scrutiny because the profit motive intersects with essential public services.

### The Carried Interest Tax Debate

In most jurisdictions, carried interest is taxed as capital gains (at rates of 15–28%) rather than ordinary income (at rates of 37–45%). Critics argue this is unjustified — carry is compensation for labour (managing investments), not return on invested capital. Defenders argue that carry aligns with long-term capital risk-taking and that changing the treatment would distort incentive structures.

This debate has persisted in US, UK, and EU policy discussions for over 20 years without resolution. PE professionals should understand the arguments on both sides.
