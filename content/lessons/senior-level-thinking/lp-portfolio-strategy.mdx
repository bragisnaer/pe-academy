Understanding PE from the LP's perspective — how institutional investors construct and manage their PE portfolios — provides a complete view of the asset class that purely GP-focused education misses.

### PE Allocation Decisions

Institutional LPs typically allocate 5–20% of their total portfolio to private equity:

- **Large public pension funds** (CalPERS, Ontario Teachers', ABP, ATP): 5–15% PE allocation
- **University endowments** (Yale, Harvard, Princeton): 15–40% PE allocation (the "endowment model")
- **Sovereign wealth funds** (ADIA, GIC, Temasek): 10–25% PE allocation
- **Insurance companies**: 2–10% PE allocation (constrained by capital adequacy requirements)

The allocation decision depends on: the LP's return objectives, liquidity needs, risk tolerance, and ability to manage the complexity of PE (monitoring, capital call management, reporting).

### Portfolio Construction

A well-constructed LP PE portfolio diversifies across:

- **Strategy** — buyout, growth equity, credit, infrastructure, secondaries
- **Geography** — North America, Europe, Asia, emerging markets
- **Vintage year** — committing capital across multiple years to avoid concentration in a single market cycle
- **GP type** — mega-fund, upper mid-market, mid-market, sector specialist
- **Fund size** — larger funds offer lower risk and lower return; smaller funds offer higher return potential but greater dispersion

### The Commitment Pacing Model

Because PE capital is called over time (not invested all at once), LPs must manage **commitment pacing** — the rate at which they commit to new funds — to maintain their target PE allocation.

If an LP wants to maintain a 10% PE allocation on a $50 billion total portfolio ($5 billion in PE), it must commit approximately $1.5–2 billion per year to new funds (accounting for the fact that existing commitments are being drawn down and older funds are distributing capital).

Under-committing leaves the LP below target allocation (missing return opportunities). Over-committing can create liquidity stress when multiple funds call capital simultaneously — a risk that materialised for some LPs during the 2008 financial crisis.
