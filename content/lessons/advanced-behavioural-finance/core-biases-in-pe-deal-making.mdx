### Confirmation Bias

**What it is:** The tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms your pre-existing belief — and to ignore or discount information that contradicts it.

**How it appears in PE:** A deal team that has developed an investment thesis over weeks of work will unconsciously filter due diligence findings to support the thesis. Positive customer interview feedback is emphasised. Negative feedback is rationalised ("that customer is an outlier"). Financial red flags are explained away rather than investigated.

**The danger:** The team presents a biased view to the investment committee, which approves a deal that a more balanced analysis would have flagged.

**Countermeasures:**
- Assign a formal "devil's advocate" or "red team" role in every IC process — a senior person whose explicit job is to challenge the thesis
- Require the deal team to present the strongest bear case before presenting the bull case
- Track IC outcomes and post-mortem unsuccessful investments to identify patterns of confirmation bias

### Anchoring

**What it is:** The tendency to rely disproportionately on the first piece of information encountered (the "anchor") when making decisions.

**How it appears in PE:** The seller's asking price becomes the anchor. Even if the buyer's independent analysis suggests a lower valuation, the negotiation gravitates toward the seller's number. Similarly, the initial financial model (built with early, optimistic assumptions) becomes an anchor that subsequent analysis adjusts only incrementally rather than fundamentally.

**The danger:** Overpaying for acquisitions because the buyer's valuation is unconsciously influenced by the seller's framing.

**Countermeasures:**
- Build the financial model independently before reviewing the seller's information memorandum pricing
- Use multiple valuation methodologies (trading comps, deal comps, DCF, LBO) and let the convergence zone define the price, not the seller's anchor
- In negotiations, set your own anchor early (a well-justified indicative offer) rather than reacting to the seller's number

### Sunk Cost Fallacy

**What it is:** The tendency to continue investing in a decision because of the resources already committed, rather than evaluating the decision based on future costs and benefits alone.

**How it appears in PE:** A deal team has spent 3 months and €800,000 in advisory fees on a transaction. Diligence reveals a significant issue. The rational response may be to walk away — but the team is emotionally and financially invested. The conversation shifts from "should we do this deal?" to "how can we make this deal work?"

**The danger:** Completing transactions that should have been abandoned, leading to underperforming investments.

**Countermeasures:**
- Create explicit "kill criteria" at the start of every deal process — specific findings that would cause the deal to be abandoned, agreed before diligence costs are incurred
- Make walk-away decisions at the partner/IC level, not the deal team level (the deal team has the most sunk cost investment)
- Track and celebrate kill decisions — firms that treat walking away as a sign of discipline rather than failure will kill more bad deals

### Winner's Curse

**What it is:** In a competitive auction, the winning bidder is by definition the bidder who valued the asset most highly — which in many cases means the bidder who overestimated the asset's value.

**How it appears in PE:** In a competitive M&A auction with 5–10 bidders, the winning bid may reflect optimistic assumptions (aggressive revenue growth, full synergy realisation, high exit multiple) that are not achievable. The winner "wins" the auction but overpays.

**The danger:** Overpaying in competitive processes, compressing returns before the hold period even begins.

**Countermeasures:**
- Set a maximum price (based on the LBO model's ability-to-pay analysis) before entering the final round and do not exceed it
- Build downside returns at the proposed bid price: if the downside still generates an acceptable return, the price is defensible
- Be willing to lose auctions. Top-performing PE firms lose more auctions than they win because they maintain pricing discipline

### Groupthink

**What it is:** The tendency for a group to converge on a consensus view, particularly when a dominant individual (the senior partner) signals their opinion early.

**How it appears in PE:** In investment committee meetings, junior team members may be reluctant to challenge the senior partner's enthusiasm for a deal. Dissenting views are suppressed because disagreeing with the boss carries career risk. The IC rubber-stamps the deal rather than subjecting it to genuine challenge.

**The danger:** Poor investment decisions that would have been caught if the IC process genuinely tested the thesis.

**Countermeasures:**
- The most senior person speaks last. Junior team members present their views first, before the senior partner signals a preference.
- Anonymous pre-meeting votes: each IC member submits a written assessment (proceed, proceed with conditions, or reject) before the meeting begins
- External IC members: some firms include external advisers or industry experts on the IC to provide an independent perspective

### Overconfidence

**What it is:** The tendency to overestimate one's own knowledge, predictive ability, or control over outcomes.

**How it appears in PE:** Overconfident deal teams overestimate revenue growth, underestimate implementation timelines for operational improvements, and assume exit multiples will expand. Overconfidence is particularly dangerous in firms with strong recent track records — success breeds belief that the team's judgement is better than it actually is.

**The danger:** Overly aggressive operating plans, excessive leverage, and insufficient contingency planning.

**Countermeasures:**
- Require base case assumptions to be independently validated (by CDD advisers, FDD advisers, or operating partners) rather than accepted from the deal team
- Benchmark operating assumptions against historical performance of comparable portfolio companies (what margin improvement was actually achieved in past deals, not what was planned?)
- Review the "planning fallacy" — projects and improvements consistently take longer and cost more than projected. Haircut implementation timelines by 30–50%

### Loss Aversion

**What it is:** The tendency to feel losses more intensely than equivalent gains. Losing €1 million feels worse than gaining €1 million feels good.

**How it appears in PE:** Portfolio companies that are underperforming are held longer than they should be because the GP is reluctant to crystallise a loss. The GP invests additional capital ("doubling down") in the hope of recovery, even when the rational analysis suggests cutting losses.

**The danger:** "Good money after bad." Holding underperformers too long ties up fund capital that could be redeployed, and additional investment in a deteriorating business may not be recoverable.

**Countermeasures:**
- Set clear performance thresholds for portfolio companies at entry: if EBITDA falls below €X or leverage exceeds Y, a formal review is triggered
- Separate the "hold or sell" decision from the deal team that made the original investment (they have the strongest emotional attachment)
- Review every portfolio company annually with the question: "if we did not already own this business, would we buy it today at its current valuation?" If the answer is no, consider exiting
