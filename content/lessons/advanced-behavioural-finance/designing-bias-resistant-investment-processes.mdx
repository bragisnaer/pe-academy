Individual awareness of biases is necessary but insufficient. The real countermeasure is process design — building investment processes that structurally reduce the influence of biases on decisions.

### Pre-Commitment Devices

- Define kill criteria before diligence begins
- Set maximum bid prices before final round negotiations
- Establish minimum return thresholds that cannot be adjusted mid-process

### Structured Disagreement

- Assign a formal red team for every IC presentation
- Require written dissent from IC members who disagree with the majority
- Track the accuracy of red team concerns versus actual deal outcomes to calibrate the process

### Decision Journals

Some senior PE investors maintain decision journals — written records at the time of each investment decision documenting:
- What was the thesis?
- What were the key assumptions?
- What would need to be true for the thesis to fail?
- What biases might be influencing this decision?

Reviewing the journal 2–3 years later reveals patterns of bias that are invisible in the moment.

### Post-Mortem Reviews

After every exit (whether successful or not), conduct a structured post-mortem:
- Did the thesis play out as expected? If not, why?
- Were the key risks identified during diligence the ones that actually materialised?
- Were there risks that were missed entirely?
- Were there moments during the hold period when the GP should have acted differently?
- What biases were present in the original investment decision?

Firms that conduct honest post-mortems compound their learning over fund generations. Firms that do not repeat the same mistakes.

### Exercise: Bias Identification

**Task:** Read the following IC scenario and identify every bias present.

"The deal team has been working on this healthcare services acquisition for 4 months. The senior partner is enthusiastic — he personally sourced the deal through his network and introduced the management team. The team has spent €1.2M on diligence fees. Financial DD flagged that adjusted EBITDA includes a €3M add-back for 'non-recurring' regulatory compliance costs that have appeared in 3 of the last 4 years, but the deal team believes these will not recur because the regulatory environment has stabilised. Commercial DD customer interviews were mixed — 8 of 12 customers were positive, but the 4 negative responses cited increasing competition and price pressure. The team's base case projects 12% revenue growth, which exceeds the company's historical average of 7%. The exit multiple assumption is 13x, compared to the 10x entry multiple. The deal team presented the bull case first and spent 35 minutes on it; the bear case was covered in 5 minutes at the end."

**Analysis:**
1. **Sunk cost fallacy** — €1.2M in fees and 4 months of work create pressure to proceed
2. **Anchoring / ownership bias** — the senior partner personally sourced the deal and introduced management; his ego is tied to the outcome
3. **Confirmation bias** — negative customer feedback (4 of 12) is not weighted equally to positive feedback; the regulatory add-back is rationalised rather than investigated
4. **Overconfidence** — 12% revenue growth projection exceeds the 7% historical average without clear justification
5. **Anchoring on exit** — assuming 13x exit from a 10x entry relies on multiple expansion of 3 turns, which is aggressive
6. **Groupthink risk** — the senior partner's enthusiasm, combined with the presentation structure (bull case first, bear case rushed), signals to the IC that the "right" answer is to approve
7. **Framing bias** — presenting the bull case first and the bear case briefly at the end frames the deal positively and minimises scrutiny of downside scenarios

### Key Terms Introduced in This Module

- **Confirmation bias** — seeking information that supports a pre-existing belief while discounting contradictory evidence
- **Anchoring** — over-relying on the first piece of information encountered (e.g., the seller's asking price)
- **Sunk cost fallacy** — continuing with a decision because of resources already committed rather than evaluating future costs and benefits
- **Winner's curse** — in competitive auctions, the winning bidder tends to be the one who overestimated the asset's value
- **Groupthink** — a group converging on a consensus view, suppressing dissent
- **Loss aversion** — feeling losses more intensely than equivalent gains, leading to holding losers too long
- **Pre-mortem** — a structured exercise imagining a deal has failed and identifying the most likely causes before committing
- **Decision journal** — a written record of investment decisions and the reasoning behind them, reviewed later to identify bias patterns
