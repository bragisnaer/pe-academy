A private equity acquisition is not a single event — it is a structured process unfolding over six to twelve months, involving dozens of professionals across multiple organisations. Each stage serves a specific purpose: narrowing risk, validating the investment thesis, and ultimately arriving at a price and structure that works for both buyer and seller.

Understanding the deal process end-to-end is one of the most practical things a PE professional can learn. Every firm's process differs in detail, but the broad arc is consistent across the industry.

## Stage 1: Sourcing

Every deal begins with sourcing — identifying potential acquisition targets that fit the firm's investment criteria. PE firms source deals through several channels:

- **Proprietary outreach** — directly approaching company owners or management teams without a formal sale process; often produces better pricing and less competition
- **Investment bank intermediaries** — banks running formal sale processes (auctions) on behalf of sellers; more competitive but a reliable deal flow source
- **Industry relationships** — introductions through portfolio company management, operating advisers, or sector consultants
- **Deal databases and market monitoring** — tracking ownership transitions, management changes, and corporate carve-outs

Proprietary deal flow — deals where the PE firm is the only or primary buyer — is a significant competitive advantage. Firms with deep sector expertise and long-standing industry relationships source disproportionately more off-market opportunities.

## Stage 2: Initial Screening

Not every sourced opportunity becomes a serious investment process. The deal team applies a rapid screen against the firm's investment criteria:

- Is the business in a sector the firm knows well?
- Does it meet minimum EBITDA, revenue, or growth thresholds?
- Is the valuation expectation in a range that can generate target returns?
- Is the ownership situation (founder, corporate parent, PE-backed) workable?

A deal that passes initial screening moves to a more detailed preliminary assessment — often involving a brief financial model and a two-page summary for senior partner review.

## Stage 3: Due Diligence

**Due diligence** is the most labour-intensive phase of the deal process. The deal team, supported by external advisers, investigates the target business in depth across four dimensions:

- **Commercial due diligence** — market size, competitive dynamics, customer concentration, pricing power, and growth drivers. Often led by a management consulting firm.
- **Financial due diligence** — quality of earnings analysis (stripping out non-recurring items to arrive at true underlying EBITDA), working capital profile, capex requirements, and historical cash flow. Led by an accounting firm.
- **Legal due diligence** — review of material contracts, intellectual property, litigation, regulatory status, and employment agreements. Led by a law firm.
- **Operational due diligence** — assessment of management team capability, IT systems, supply chain, and operational improvement opportunities.

The **letter of intent (LOI)** is typically signed before or during confirmatory due diligence. The LOI is a non-binding document outlining the proposed purchase price, deal structure, and exclusivity period — usually 30–60 days during which the seller cannot negotiate with other buyers.

## Stage 4: Investment Committee Approval

Midway through due diligence, the deal team presents to the **investment committee (IC)** for approval to proceed. The IC presentation covers:

- The investment thesis and value creation plan
- Financial model with base case, upside, and downside scenarios
- Due diligence findings and key risks
- Proposed capital structure (equity vs. debt) and valuation
- Proposed exit strategy and target returns

IC approval is not always binary — the committee may approve with conditions, require additional diligence, or request a revised price before proceeding.

## Stage 5: Financing

For **leveraged buyouts**, securing debt financing runs in parallel with due diligence. The deal team works with investment banks or direct lenders to structure the debt package:

- Determining the appropriate **leverage ratio** (total debt as a multiple of EBITDA — typically 4–7x for mid-market deals)
- Negotiating debt terms: interest rate, covenants, amortisation schedule, and prepayment flexibility
- Receiving credit committee approval from the lending institution

In competitive processes, the ability to move quickly on financing — and to credibly commit to closing — is a significant advantage.

## Stage 6: Definitive Agreement and Closing

Once due diligence is complete and financing is committed, the parties negotiate and sign a **definitive agreement** — the Sale and Purchase Agreement (SPA). The SPA governs all terms of the transaction: price, representations and warranties, conditions to closing, indemnification obligations, and post-closing adjustments.

The period between signing and closing (sometimes days, sometimes months) allows for regulatory approvals, third-party consents, and satisfaction of any closing conditions.

## Stage 7: Post-Acquisition and Value Creation

The deal closes, ownership transfers, and the real work begins. The PE firm, working with management, executes the value creation plan — which might involve:

- **Operational improvement** — cost reduction, procurement optimisation, pricing improvements
- **Revenue growth** — geographic expansion, new product lines, sales force investment
- **Bolt-on acquisitions** — acquiring complementary businesses to add scale, capabilities, or market share
- **Management strengthening** — recruiting additional talent where gaps exist

The hold period — typically 3–7 years — is when the thesis is tested against reality.

## Stage 8: Exit

The **exit strategy** is the final stage, converting the equity investment back into cash for distribution to LPs. Common exit routes include:

- **Trade sale** — selling to a strategic buyer (a corporation in the same industry)
- **Secondary buyout** — selling to another PE firm
- **IPO** — listing the company on a public stock exchange
- **Recapitalisation** — refinancing debt to pay a dividend without selling the business outright

The exit valuation minus the original investment (plus any interim distributions) determines the deal's MOIC and IRR — the metrics by which the investment's success is ultimately judged.

## Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Letter of intent (LOI)** — a non-binding document outlining proposed deal terms and granting the buyer exclusivity to complete due diligence
- **Due diligence** — comprehensive investigation of a target company across commercial, financial, legal, and operational dimensions before acquisition
- **Exit strategy** — the planned method by which a PE firm will ultimately sell its investment and return capital to LPs
- **Bolt-on acquisition** — a smaller acquisition made after the initial platform investment to add scale, capabilities, or market access
- **Leverage ratio** — total debt as a multiple of EBITDA; a key measure of financial risk in an LBO capital structure
