The **leveraged buyout (LBO)** is the defining transaction structure of private equity. Almost everything distinctive about PE — the use of debt, the focus on cash flow, the concentrated ownership, the 5-year hold period — flows directly from the mechanics of the LBO. Understanding how an LBO works is therefore foundational to understanding the entire asset class.

## What Is a Leveraged Buyout?

A **leveraged buyout** is the acquisition of a company using a combination of equity (the PE firm's fund capital) and a significant amount of borrowed money (debt). The acquired company's own assets and future cash flows serve as collateral for the debt.

A typical LBO capital structure might look like this:

> **Example:** Acquiring a company for $500 million (10x **EBITDA** of $50 million)
> - Debt: $325 million (65% of purchase price, ~6.5x EBITDA)
> - Equity: $175 million (35% of purchase price)

The PE firm contributes $175 million of fund capital. The remaining $325 million comes from lenders — banks or direct lenders — who are repaid from the company's cash flows over the investment period.

## Why Use Leverage?

Leverage amplifies equity returns — this is the core mathematical logic of the LBO.

Consider two scenarios for the same $500 million acquisition:

**Without leverage (all equity):** PE firm invests $500 million. Company is sold 5 years later for $750 million. Return = 1.5x MOIC.

**With leverage (65% debt):** PE firm invests $175 million equity. Company is sold for $750 million. After repaying $275 million of remaining debt (assuming $50 million was paid down), equity receives $475 million. Return = 2.7x MOIC.

Same business, same exit price — but leverage transforms the equity return from 1.5x to 2.7x. This is the **leverage effect**: debt amplifies gains on the equity invested.

The flip side is equally important: leverage also amplifies losses. If the business deteriorates and is sold for $350 million, the all-equity investor loses $150 million (30% loss). The leveraged investor may lose their entire $175 million equity investment if debt can't be fully repaid.

This asymmetry is why **EBITDA** — earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortisation — is the central metric in LBO analysis. It approximates the cash a business generates to service debt. A company with $50 million of stable, growing EBITDA can safely support far more debt than a company with $50 million of volatile, cyclical EBITDA.

## The Ideal LBO Candidate

Not every business is suitable for a leveraged buyout. PE firms look for specific characteristics that make a company a strong LBO candidate:

- **Stable, predictable cash flows** — debt must be serviced every quarter regardless of economic conditions; businesses with recurring revenue (subscriptions, long-term contracts) are preferred
- **Strong market position** — market leadership provides pricing power and competitive moat, reducing downside risk
- **Low capital expenditure requirements** — businesses that generate cash without requiring heavy reinvestment can use that cash to repay debt faster
- **Identifiable value creation opportunities** — cost reduction, operational improvement, or growth initiatives that the PE firm can execute post-acquisition
- **Strong management team** — or a clear plan to strengthen it; operational execution is critical in a highly leveraged business
- **Clear exit path** — strategic acquirers or public markets that will pay a higher multiple than the entry price

Classic LBO candidates include software companies with subscription revenue, healthcare services businesses with stable demand, and specialised industrial companies with dominant market positions in niche sectors.

## The Capital Structure

LBO debt comes in layers (tranches), each with different risk profiles, interest rates, and repayment priority:

- **Senior secured debt** — first priority claim on assets; lowest interest rate; typically provided by banks or syndicated across institutional lenders
- **Unitranche debt** — a single blended facility combining senior and junior debt; increasingly common from direct lenders; simpler structure but higher interest rate than pure senior debt
- **Mezzanine debt** — subordinated to senior debt; higher interest rate; may include equity warrants (right to buy company shares) as additional compensation to lenders

The **debt-to-equity ratio** (or **leverage ratio** expressed as debt/EBITDA) measures how aggressively the acquisition is financed. A 6x leverage ratio means the company has 6 times its annual EBITDA in debt. Lenders, regulators, and PE firms all monitor this ratio closely as an indicator of financial risk.

## How LBOs Generate Returns

PE returns from an LBO come from three sources:

**1. EBITDA growth** — if the business grows its earnings from $50 million to $70 million over 5 years, it is simply worth more at exit (at the same valuation multiple).

**2. Multiple expansion** — if the business was acquired at 10x EBITDA and sold at 12x EBITDA (because it is now larger, more diversified, or market conditions are more favourable), the exit valuation rises even without earnings growth.

**3. Debt paydown** — as the company uses its cash flows to repay debt, the equity value increases mechanically. If debt falls from $325 million to $200 million while enterprise value stays constant, equity value rises from $175 million to $300 million.

The best LBO returns combine all three: a business that grows EBITDA, is sold at a higher multiple, and has repaid significant debt during the hold period.

## Key Terms Introduced in This Lesson

- **Leveraged buyout (LBO)** — acquisition of a company using a combination of equity and significant borrowed debt, with the acquired company's cash flows servicing the debt
- **EBITDA** — earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortisation; the primary cash flow proxy used in LBO analysis and valuation
- **Leverage ratio** — total debt divided by EBITDA; a measure of how heavily a company is financed with borrowed money
- **Debt-to-equity ratio** — the proportion of debt versus equity in a company's capital structure; higher ratios amplify both returns and risk
- **Multiple expansion** — an increase in the valuation multiple (e.g. EV/EBITDA) between acquisition and exit, contributing to equity returns independent of earnings growth
